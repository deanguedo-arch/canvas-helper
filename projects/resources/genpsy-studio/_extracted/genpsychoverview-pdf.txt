 Section 2:  The Principles of Learning
Objectives:
• To understand and explain the basic concepts of the learning process
1. General Overview of Learning
Learning is very complex.  You spend twelve years in school and learn only a fraction of
what you need to know.  This assignment is packed with information and terms, including
the following basic ideas.
What is learning?
• Learning is mastering information.  It is the ability to acquire and process the
information we need.
•   We acquire some abilities “naturally” through the process of maturation rather
than learning.  This means as our body matures we
are able to do tasks such as cut with
scissors—something an infant is not yet able to do.
• Motivation is an important factor in learning.
When we are motivated, learning becomes more
efficient and more effective.
Principles of Efficient Learning (text, pages 38 and 39)
• Be mentally ready.
• Be focused and determined.
• Use organized material.  (Advanced organizers
are packaged summaries of information that will
follow.)
• Use periodic reviews.
• Use self-recitation of information.
• Bring positive emotions to the learning situation.
Other elements of learning to keep in mind:
• Overlearning:  Information is studied and reviewed repeatedly until we know
it thoroughly and remember it for a long time.  Is overlearning related to
“cramming” in any way?  What are diminishing returns?  (text, page 31)
• Mnemonic devices may help us remember information, such as the ABC’s song.
(text, page 34)
• In some situations, learners resort to trial-and-error learning in which they more
or less guess a series of answers until they find the correct one accidentally.

Objectives:
•   To understand the significance of memory to learning
• To be aware of how the brain processes information
• To understanding the foundations of thinking and reasoning

1. The Memory
Begin with some information about memory that is a critical part of thinking.
• Could you have thoughts without memory?
The text declares that a person’s earliest memories occur after a young child is able to
talk and put together ideas verbally.
• What are your first memories of childhood?  How old were you when you had
these memories?
Memory is the foundation of thought and occurs only after we are verbal or use
language.
2. The Brain
The brain has two important functions:
• To analyze information
• To create ideas
Think about it.
Which of the above functions have you used to process the
information you have just been given about Assignment 3?
The brain is responsible for a wide range of complex procedures.  The brain
is divided into two compartments or hemispheres.  The two divisions are well
integrated, but people have a preferred hemisphere.
Left-brain hemisphere specializes in language, logical thought, verbal expression,
numbers, and the processing of data.
Right-brain hemisphere specializes in the whole picture, intuition, creativity, visual
awareness, feelings, body movement, and the random processing of data.

Every task requires tools and equipment.  Tools for thinking involve language but also
symbols.  Thinking is the mental process and skills we use to shape our lives.
Language is important to us in four key ways:
• Language helps us remember past events.
• Language sharpens our awareness of what we
experience.
• Language builds unity and connections with others.
• Language gives direction to our behaviour.
Three main thinking processes:
Remembering, which involves• Sensory (the senses)
• Motor skills (physical)
• Verbal (language)
Reasoning, which can be•   Inductive - specific/general

Critical Thinking, marked by a desire to•   Be reflective

•     Challenge
• Shift opinions
Thinking enables people to remember information, use reasoning to form judgments
about the data, and be critical thinkers, which involves one’s attitude about the
worthiness of the information.

Four reasons for forgetting:
• Information was not completely learned in the beginning.
• New information is introduced, which may cause original data to be forgotten.
• Prior learning is so strongly ingrained that it interferes with new data.
• Emotional upheaval is present; anxiety surrounding the learning situation causes
forgetting.
4. Major Problems
Communication Problems:  Dyslexia
This language disability can affect reading, math skills, writing, and speaking.
Concentration Problems:  ADHD (Attention Deficit Disorder)
The common characteristics are poor attention, focus, and listening along with
hyperactivity and the tendency to interrupt.
Interrelating Problems:  Autism
This neurological brain disorder affects behaviour, communication, and social
interactions.  Information processing is heavily affected, leading to self-imposed
isolation.
EXTRA:  Colour
Colour has an important role in receiving information from the outside world.  It affects
our lives in many ways, especially through our quality of thoughts, our moods, and our
energy levels.

2. Group Behaviour
Individual behaviour can undergo great changes when the individual becomes a member
of a group.  Because we are social beings, we are attracted to the idea of being part of a
larger network of similar beings.  Some comfort comes from being part of a group.
Examine the following scenarios to understand the influence of various groups.
Crowd:  You go to an afternoon comedy movie and are the only patron in the theatre.
You go to an evening comedy movie packed with people who laugh through the whole
movie.  Does your behaviour change?
Clique:  You go to the mall alone for an afternoon of shopping knowing exactly what you
want to buy.  You go to the mall with your close group of friends finding yourself trying
on various outfits and noticing your friends’ reactions.  Do your purchases change?
Inclusive Group:  Your grad group decides to have a car wash to raise money.  You try to
recruit as many people as possible to help.  Would you be choosey about who joined the
team?
Exclusive Group:  You have five close friends from school who have hung out together
for years.  Some new people show an interest in joining your special group of friends.
What things would concern you about this change?
Risky Shift:  As leader of the fund-raising group, you make a decision to spend a lot of
money on advertising.  This puts your budget close to the edge.  Last year the group took
a big chance on a fund-raising idea, and everyone was comfortable with it.  What factors
would influence your decision?
Cautious Shift:  As leader of a group, you decide not to pay a large fee for a DJ at the
dance because you do not want group members to suffer financially.  Many come from
homes where parents are struggling with financial issues.  What factors would influence
your decision?

Objectives:
• To understand the nature of emotions and how they effect our lives
• To differentiate the types of defence mechanisms
• To have knowledge of the criminal and unhealthy elements of emotions
1. Basic Elements of Emotions
Emotions paint our behaviour with colour and energy.  This unit deals with many
elements of emotions.  In the following situations are two distinctive perspectives for
each point of information.
• Origin of Aggression:  One of our most problematic emotions is aggression.
◦ Is aggression inherited or inborn because it is so closely linked to our struggle
to survive?
◦ Or, is aggression learned from watching others and copying their behaviour
that arises from frustration?
• Aggression—Bad/Good:  On the negative side, is very aggressive behaviour
characterized by violent action.  One of the most abhorrent forms of aggression
is abuse against children.  On the positive side, aggressive behaviour may be
assertive, which means that people are “standing up for their rights”.
• Frustration—Circumstances/People:  Many situations produce reactions of
frustration.  Sometimes, serious obstacles keep us from our goals.  Sometimes,
we have “difficult people” who we must work with and who do not respond to
situations the way we do.
• Approach/Avoidance:  Think of combinations of positive and negative situations
in your life, and think of examples for the following:
◦ approach/approach
◦ avoidance/avoidance
◦ approach/avoidance
• Stress—Good/Bad:  Stress has a positive side, making people alert, responsive,
and resourceful.  However, stress can lead to various illnesses, including
psychosomatic illness, as our bodies work under the burden of pressure.  Suicide
is a special case of negative behaviour.  It requires support from friends and
family, but also help from professionals.  Stress can affect physical health, mental
clarity, and emotional control.

3. The Criminal and Severely Deviant Side
• Crime Rates:  Although the perception is that crime rates are out of control, statistics
show them very stable for many major crimes.  Our news media inform us of these
events daily, even hourly, which was not so in the past.  This can make crimes seem
more prevalent.
• Personal Consequences of Crime:  Victims of crimes have many physical,
emotional, and psychological difficulties to overcome before they can heal and
become fully functioning members of society again.  Victim trauma syndrome is an
intense reaction to abuse resulting from a crime.
When violence invades the home as spousal abuse, the effects are very serious
(possibly life threatening) to many members of the family.  Battered wife syndrome is
an overpowering fear that may push the victim to a violent response.
• Media and Violence:  Violence constantly portrayed in the media and now in
video games can have very negative affects on those immersed in it.  People fail to
connect with the pain and suffering inflicted on the victim, a phenomenon known as
desensitization.
• Zero Tolerance Policy:  This policy attempts to eliminate aggressive, violent acts
towards others by forbidding them.  In the school setting, this means that students
can enter a learning environment without fear of bullies or others behaving in
inappropriate, aggressive ways.
• Young Offenders Act:  Many citizens believe the structure of the Young Offenders
Act must be reviewed.
◦ What was the purpose behind the legislation?
◦ Why do many people believe it is being misused?
• Deviance through Stealing:  Kleptomania is stealing urged by psychology motives
such as revenge and sexual excitement.
• Deviance through Fires:  Pyromania is the compulsive urge to set fires for reasons of
defiance, hostility, and sexual motives.
• Deviance from Psychopaths:  Psychopaths continually participate in antisocial
activities because they have no conscience, they show no remorse for wrongdoing,
and they are charming liars and predators.  They have no moral principles.

You were given two different and distinctive definitions of abnormal behaviour.  It’s
important to use them correctly.
Mentally challenged describes an individual with a physical impairment that shows lack
of full mental development.  A person with Down’s Syndrome is mentally challenged.
Mental illness describes an individual who has faulty reasoning and is out of touch with
reality.  A person with schizophrenia or bipolar disorder has a mental illness.
2.  Neurosis
Neurosis is a psychological disorder characterized by
anxiety, sometimes guilt, and depression.  Remember
that the underlying problem is excessive anxiety.
Neurotic behaviour is based on faulty emotional
habits.
The anxiety that the neurotic person experiences
may be bound, which means it is linked to a specific
object or event.  However, sometimes anxiety is
free-floating.  This means the person can experience
unpredictable, general anxiety, unrelated to a specific
“trigger”.
The information in the booklet is not presented in this order, but neurotic problems
seem to group themselves in three major categories:  physically based, mentally
based, and emotionally based.  Any neurotic reaction has some element of each one,
but this classification considers the dominant factor
Neurosis:  Physically-Based Reactions
• Psychosomatic Disorders:  physical (bodily) illness that occurs as a result of
emotional stress
• Conversion Hysteria:  a physical symptom that occurs in response to energy
released from a repressed emotion
These illnesses are like getting an excuse slip that keeps us from fearful situations.
• Anorexia Nervosa:  appetite control to the point of starvation and sometimes
death
The issue behind this situation is “control” through restriction of food.

2. General Description of Pyschosis
Psychosis is a serious pathological state resulting in the loss of contact with reality.
What does this mean?
A psychotic person may display these behaviours:
• is out of touch with reality
• may be partially or completely withdrawn from others
• holds confused ideas about people
•     has     delusions (false beliefs) or hallucinations (false
sensory perceptions)
Predisposition may have a big part in the development of psychotic behaviour.
Two major elements are involved:
• a tendency to develop a condition (inherited)
• a factor in the environment that may trigger the behaviour to develop
The Stigma Judgment:  Psychotic conditions are often misunderstood and sometimes
feared.  People often attach feelings of shame or disgrace to psychotic behaviour.
• What can we do to dissolve any stigma?  (Remember that many stigmas are
undeserved.)

4. Types of Treatments
Details of the three basic groups of treatments are given in text, pages 226-230.
a. Medical Module:  treats people’s mental illness through physical means to correct a
physical problem that is causing a psychological problem
• drug treatment (ataractics)
• insulin shock (re-establishes normal brain patterns)
• faradic stimulation (once was electroconvulsive shock)
• prefrontal lobotomy (no longer used)
b. Psychosocial Module:  treats people’s mental illness through psychological means
such as mending relationships and focusing on the person’s social environment
• group therapy (empathy and support of others)
• hypnotherapy (hypnosis)
• psychoanalysis (search for repressed emotions)
• play therapy (used with children)
• sleep deprivation (accelerates response to antidepressant drugs)
c. Eclectic Approach:  treats people’s mental illness through a combination of theories
and techniques tailored to an individual’s particular problem
