Point of clarification regarding sensation and perception:

Sensation refers to the process of receiving stimuli from the environment using one of the five
senses and then having that information forwarded to our brains for processing.  Perception is
the processing and interpretation of the sensory stimuli we receive to better understand our
environment.

Point of clarification regarding cellular layers in the retina:

Page 113 incorrectly states that light first goes through the photoreceptor layer (rods and cones)
then the bipolar layer and finally the ganglion cell layer.  What actually happens is that light
entering the eye passes through the ganglion cell layer then the plexiform and nuclear layers
(with bipolar cells) on its way to the photoreceptor layer where the rods and cones are located.
The rods and cones then activate and send signals back to the ganglion cells through the bipolar
cells.

Corrections to Course Content Regarding the Five Senses

 Sight:  The diagram on page 113 incorrectly
indicates that the blind spot is located in the vitreous
humour at the back of the eye just in front of the
retina.  The blind spot is actually located where the
optic nerve enters/exits the retina.

 Sound:  The diagram on page 125 depicts sound waves as transverse waves (like light
waves) with high and low points (crests and troughs) in the waves.  Sound, in fact, is a
longitudinal wave.  A correct diagram of longitudinal waves formed when a tuning fork
is struck near a pipe with air in it is as follows:

o The distance between two consecutive areas of compression (wave cycles)
determines the pitch of the sound wave.  Long distances between compressions
result a lower pitch (lower frequency).  Shorter distances between compression
result in a higher pitch (higher frequency).  Frequency is the number of
compressions (wave cycles) in a given time period.

o The number of air molecules disturbed or compacted in a compression area
relates to the sound pressure of a sound wave.  The pressure (also known as
amplitude) relates to the loudness or quietness of a sound.  Without molecules
(air, water, etc.) to transmit the energy (pressure) of a sound wave, there is no
audible sound.  In other words, if a hammer strikes a metal bar in a container that
has had all the air removed (i.e., a vacuum), you would not hear anything.

 Taste & Smell:  The course materials correctly identify that taste and smell are
intimately entwined and function together to enhance the experience of eating.  On page
126, however, the materials identify four basic tastes:  sour, salty, sweet, and bitter.
There is actually a fifth basic taste called umami.  The word umami is borrowed from the
Japanese culture and is translated as “pleasant savory taste.”  Ripe tomatoes are thought
to be rich in umami flavour!

 Touch:  This sense includes pain, temperature (hot and cold), as well as pressure.  There
are two typographical errors on page 128 that substitute “pleasure” for “pressure.”
Please note the correct term is pressure.

Addition Information Regarding Optical Illusions

Mechanisms that create optical illusions are located in various places along the visual
pathways. Some of these mechanisms come from the retina itself, but most result from the way
that the images captured with the eyes are reconstructed by the visual cortex.

The information our brain receives from our eyes doesn’t always accurately represent reality.
For example, some of the information received from the 125 million photoreceptors is lost as
the information flows to 100 times fewer ganglion cells before being transmitted to the brain
by the optic nerve.

To compensate for the loss of information, the brain introduces abstract factors that complete
or amplify the components of reality that are transmitted.  In doing so, the brain sometimes
creates an impression of unity/logic where there is none—in other words, an optical illusion.
There are many types of optical illusions including those related to depth, colour, motion, and
size.  The written-response items 1 and 2 relate to optical illusions.
