Forensic Determination of DNA Match

The STR and VNTR that are used in DNA profiling are distinct in two ways:

  1. the arrangement of the nucleic acid base pairs (for example, ATA – GTA – CAC)
  2. the number of times this distinct pattern repeats itself at a particular locus on a DNA strand

When investigators are trying to compare two samples of DNA, they look at the same locus in each sample (for example, chromosome 12 or chromosome 20).

For example, eight repeats of a particular STR are found at one locus of a suspect DNA sample, and eight repeats of this same STR are found at the same locus in a crime scene DNA sample. This could indicate that the two DNA samples came from the same person. However, you cannot make this assumption upon analysis of a single locus because there is a chance that someone else on Earth could have this same STR at the same locus. Therefore, in forensic investigations, a five-locus DNA profile is done in which VNTR/STR patterns from five different loci are examined. If a suspect's DNA shows the exact same VNTR/STR repeat patterns at all five loci as a crime scene sample, the probability that the DNA found at the scene came from someone other than the suspect is very small.

The inheritance of the VNTR/STR patterns at each locus is independent of any other locus. Therefore, the percentages must be multiplied to determine the probability of the DNA coming from someone other than the suspect. If an unusual VNTR/STR pattern has been found and analyzed in a crime scene sample and a suspect sample, the odds of another person with an identical VNTR/STR at five different loci can be staggering, perhaps 1 in 40 billion.

Determination of Population Frequency Values

To estimate the frequency of a particular DNA pattern, the number of occurrences of the pattern is counted in a random population sample. For example, if a particular VNTR/STR pattern occurred in 1 of 100 DNA samples, the estimated population frequency would be 1%.

Population frequency values used for forensic DNA typing are based on population genetic principles. Three steps occur in the calculation of these values:

  1. The number of alternate forms of a particular VNTR or STR section are calculated.
  2. The number of each of these VNTR or STR sections in a databank of DNA samples is recorded.
  3. The chance of having each of these different VNTR or STR sequences at five different loci is calculated mathematically to produce a probability ratio.

Although a databank might contain only 500 people, numerous different VNTR or STR patterns might result in an estimated population frequency of one in a billion.

The FBI has reported that in their DNA profile database, no two five-locus DNA profiles have ever matched.

In December 2005, after serving 24 years in prison, Robert Clark was proven innocent of a 1981 attack on an Atlanta woman. Clark was the 164th person in United States to be freed using post-conviction DNA analysis.
