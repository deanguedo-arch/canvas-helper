Hair Structure

When it comes to hair evidence, the forensic scientist is interested in looking at the length, diameter and internal structures - tip variation, root variation, cortex color, medullary index and medullary shape.  The best way to  observe any type of trace evidence is with the aid of a basic microscope.

The three main parts of a hair include the root, the tip and the shaft.  Variations within each of these parts can indicate some information about the individual from which it was obtained from.

1. The Hair Root

Hair grows from a follicle, which provides nutrients and oxygen and removes any wastes from the hair root.  The only part of the hair that requires oxygen and nutrients is the hair root as the cells in the rest of the hair is non-living.  The hair root lies below the epidermis layer of the skin and is similar in appearance to a tulip bulb given its rounded shape.

Some significant variations in the hair root many help a forensic scientist determine if the hair came from a human or an animal and how the hair became dislodged.  For example, did it fall out  naturally or was it forcefully removed in a violent struggle?

Rounded (club) root: This indicates that the hair fell out naturally.


Follicle-attached root: If the follicle is attached to the root (the follicle looks like transparent skin around the root) one can deduce that the hair was forcibly removed, perhaps during some sort of struggle.


Frayed Root: This indicates that the hair likely came from a cat.


Spade-shaped root: This indicates that the hair likely came from a dog.


2. The Hair Tip

The tip of the hair can be distinguished from the root as tips will never have any skin directly attached to them.  This part of the hair is not directly attached to the body.  The tip of a single hair is found furthest away from the scalp.

Slightly rounded tip: This indicates that the individual has not had a hair cut in a long period of time (likely longer than 4 weeks)

Linear/straight tip: This indicates that the individual has recently had a hair cut.

Blackened/frayed tip: This indicates that the individual has come into close contact with flames/high heat.


3. The Hair Shaft

The shaft of the hair is the region found between the root and the tip of the hair.  The three main parts of the hair shaft include the cuticle, cortex and medulla.  

The outermost layer of the hair shaft is called the cuticle, which is made up of a layer of scales covering the outside of the hair shaft.  


Imbricate cuticle: This indicates that the hair sample came from a human

Coronal cuticle: This indicates that the hair sample likely came from a dog or a rodent (i.e. mouse, rat, vole)

Spinous cuticle: This indicates that the hair likely came from a cat

The center/middle of the hair shaft is called the medulla.  The medulla shows great variation among people and species.  Under high magnification, the basic internal structure of the medulla is referred to as the medulla index.  Some of the major medulla indices include vacuolated, unisereal, lattice and amorphous (further divided into fragmented and continuous).


Unisereal medulla: This indicates that the hair is likely from a cat

Vacuolated medulla: This indicates the hair likely came from a dog

Lattice medulla: This indicates that the hair is likely from a deer or elk.

Multisereal medulla: This indicates that the hair is likely from a rabbit.

Amorphous medulla: This indicates that the sample likely came from a human.  Human with black hair tend to have continuous amorphous medullas while all other hair colors tend to have fragmented amorphous medullas.
