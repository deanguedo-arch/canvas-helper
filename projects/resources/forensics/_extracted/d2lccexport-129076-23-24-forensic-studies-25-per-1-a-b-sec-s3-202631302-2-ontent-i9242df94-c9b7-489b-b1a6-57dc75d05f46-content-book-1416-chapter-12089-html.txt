Blood is a very important part of helping to solve crimes.  Investigators often want to know whose blood they have collected from a crime scene.  There are many ways that you can identify blood at a crime scene, but most involve using the immune system.
Blood typing was first developed by Karl Landsteiner in 1901.  He developed this because he was looking for a safer way to transfuse blood to patients.  Prior to this, blood transfusion was a very dangerous and difficult task and was often shunned by the medical community.
Landsteiner noticed that when blood from two incompatible individuals was mixed, an immune reaction was triggered, resulting in the red blood cells clumping together.  By knowing this, he was able to classify blood into certain types.  Individuals all have one type of blood and the donor and receiver of blood need to have matching blood types.  Because of his discovery, blood transfusions become much safer.
This also helps forensic investigators to identify blood matches.  Blood types can be used to help identify perpetrators of crimes.  Presently, DNA fingerprinting is the gold standard for identifying blood (this will be discussed in greater detail later in the course).
