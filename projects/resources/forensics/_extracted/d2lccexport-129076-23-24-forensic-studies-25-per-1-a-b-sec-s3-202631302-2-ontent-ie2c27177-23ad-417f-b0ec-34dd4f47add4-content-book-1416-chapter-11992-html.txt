Body Fluid Evidence
This module will explore the significance of body fluid evidence found at crime scenes. The two main types of body fluids that may be found at a crime scene or upon the bodies of the individuals involved with a particular crime are blood and semen. The lessons in this module will:
outline some of the basic features of blood.
explore how latent blood residue can be enhanced and how blood spatter patterns can be interpreted.
focus upon the information that semen collected from a sexual assault yields.
examine a historical crime and a fictional crime that both involve body fluid evidence.
The body fluid that is most often found at crime scene involving injuries (ie. traffic accidents) or violence (ie. fighting, homicide) is blood. Blood evidence can help to identify an individual. Even the pattern of the blood residue (called blood spatter) can yield a great deal of information about a crime.
Semen is ejaculated by a male during sexual intercourse and this type of evidence is often collected after a sexual assault has occurred. Sometimes semen is collected from the victim’s clothing or from on or within the victim’s body. In addition, semen may be found on or near the object(s) where the sexual assault occurred (ie. bed sheets, ground/floor).
By the end of Module 4 you should be able to:
Outline the four basic blood types displayed by humans and identify the significance of a secretor in human blood types.
Explain the steps involved in the identification of an unknown blood sample through the process of blood sample testing.
Describe the relevance of the basic types of blood spatter evidence.
Outline how semen evidence is often obtained and how this evidence can help identify a culprit.
Analyze various microscopic samples of body fluids to differentiate between blood and semen.
Perform an experiment that identifies the four basic blood types and identify various unknown blood samples.
