Step-by-Step Description of DNA AnalysisTo understand how a DNA print is created, focusing upon the simpler RFLP method is most effective. Some of the basic steps involved in the RFLP method include the following. Please follow the description with the diagram below.A sample of cells is collected (e.g., blood, hair, semen, skin, saliva).Using a powerful microscope, the nucleus or mitochondria from a cell is extracted using a micropipette. (Nuclear DNA is normally used for criminal comparison while mitochondrial DNA is normally used for the identification of unrecognizable remains.  We will learn more about mitochondrial DNA in the next lesson).The chromosomes from this nucleus are isolated. Then, sections of DNA from within a chromosome are removed using a restrictive enzyme.A fragment of DNA is then placed within a device to be separated using gel electrophoresis. The DNA fragment is embedded within a thin layer of gel at one end of the device, the negative (-) pole, which has an overall negative charge. Opposite to the embedded DNA fragment is the positive (+) pole that has an overall positive charge. The positively-charged pole attracts the negatively-charged DNA fragment, and as a result the DNA slowly migrates and "stretches" through the gel to the positive pole.Once the DNA fragment has been stretched out, the DNA print is transferred onto a nylon membrane or X-ray film and preserved.Radioactive probes are applied to VNTR sections being identified in the DNA print. This makes distinct gene bands appear visible to the naked eye.Once the DNA print is complete, the analysis and comparison of the distinct gene band pattern occurs.Watch the following video for a summary of the process:
