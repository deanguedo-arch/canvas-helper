The science of chemistry will be the focus of this section as Forensic scientists use various chemical tests to confirm the presence of blood.  There are a variety of chemical tests that can be used to identify blood, but we will only examine the two most popular methods in this lesson.Trying to clean up every trace of blood after a violent crime can be next to impossible - especially if there was a great deal of blood.  Often criminals will think that they have sufficiently cleaned up a room, but end up missing minute amounts of blood spatter.  Forensic experts are aware of this and as a result one of the first things they will often do at a crime scene is to simply bring in a powerful light.  This powerful light then can be used by investigators to find any small traces of dried blood that may be left at a crime scene.  Dried blood will appear a deep burgundy or a brownish-black in color.  If an investigator does find a suspicious stain - no matter how small - a photograph is taken followed by a chemical test to confirm that it is indeed blood and not something else.The Kastle-Meyer test is used to detect blood.  This test uses phenolphthalein, which turns deep pink when exposed to hemoglobin, which is found in all red blood cells.  If an unknown stain at a crime scene is suspected to be blood, a Forensic Investigator will take a Q-tip dipped in a phenolphthalein reagent, place it upon the stain in question and then look for a bright pink color to occur.  This will indicate that the substance is indeed blood.  This chemical test is very popular for two reasons: it is fast and sensitive - it can detect blood at 1 part to 5 million.  In other words, if there is one drop of blood in a container of 4, 999, 999 drops of water, phenolphthalein will react quickly to produce a bright pink color.
