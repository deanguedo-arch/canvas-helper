A Brief History of Fingerprinting
Before a forensic fingerprint identification system was developed, criminals were classified according to various body measurements such as height, foot length, arm length, and/or head circumference.
In 1892, an English scientist, Sir Francis Galton, wrote a book called Finger Prints that discussed the unique fingerprint ridge patterns he found from person to person. Galton's work was the first of its kind. It became the foundation of modern forensic fingerprinting, but his classification system was not useful to police agencies.
Dr. Juan Vucetich, a police officer from Argentina, applied the information from Galton's book to create the first working system to sort and classify fingerprints to identify criminal suspects. Dr. Vucetich's method is still used today in Spanish-speaking countries.
In 1896, Sir Edward Henry, a British police commissioner, created and implemented a fingerprint identification and classification system at Scotland Yard. Today, police departments within most English-speaking countries use Henry's fingerprint identification system. Canada established its National Fingerprint Bureau in 1911.
With over 200 million prints on file, the Federal Bureau of Investigation in the United States has the largest collection of fingerprints in the world.
