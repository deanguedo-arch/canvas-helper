Forensic DNA Analysis

The sequencing of the four nucleic acid bases determines your genes and your genes determine what type of proteins your body creates. Every protein is created by a specific gene. Your body is made up of thousands of different proteins, and they determine your unique appearance and structure. Interestingly, only about 5% of your DNA creates the 30,000 genes that your body uses and 99% of this useful DNA is similar from person to person. Therefore, analysis of this type of DNA is NOT at all useful in forensic science. 

The function of 95% of your DNA is unknown and often referred to as junk DNA. Interestingly, this junk DNA is unique from person to person. In these sections of DNA, the nucleic acid base sequences will often repeat themselves throughout this DNA at a particular locus. For example:

T-A-G C-A-T G-G-G, T-A-G C-A-T G-G-G, T-A-G C-A-T G-G-G

Two distinctive DNA sequences that repeat themselves are used in forensic DNA profiling:

  1. Variable Number Tandem Repeats (VNTR) - 5 to 64 nucleic acid base pairs in length
  2. Short Tandem Repeats (STR) - 3 to 7 nucleic acid base pairs in length

VNTR and STR sections within DNA are the focus of a forensic DNA profile. These repetitive sequences are labeled with radioactive probes for experts to identify a match. In forensic DNA analysis, a total of five VNTR or STR patterns at five different loci have to be identical for it to be considered a match. A particular VNTR or STR pattern may be repeated 14 times at a specific location in your DNA, 20 times at the same site of your sister's DNA, and perhaps 52 times on your uncle's DNA. The odds of someone else having this exact same VNTR or STR pattern repeated the exact same number of times at five different sites in their DNA is highly improbable. Additionally, some VNTR or STR patterns are very rare.

The Wyoming Game and Fish Department (WGFD) use the analysis of animal DNA to help solve poaching crimes. The WGFD performed over 10 000 tests on 725 forensic samples as part of 95 investigations in 2004. The investigations helped the department obtain more than $224,339 in court-ordered fines.

Source: "Game and fish honored for their work." Associated Press. (October 12, 2005).
