3. Cyanoacrylate Fumigation

Cyanoacrylate is the main component of a strong glue known commercially as ‘Krazy Glue’ or ‘Super Glue’. When cyanoacrylate reacts with the body oil residue in a fingerprint, it produces a visible whitish-grey film that is especially noticeable on dark-coloured objects. This type of print cannot be lifted, so it must be photographed for analysis.

Cyanoacrylate enhances latent fingerprints that have been left upon solid objects that are smooth surface or with a slightly textured surface such as metals (such as knife handles, guns, bullet cartridges, pop cans) and hard plastics (such as credit cards, knife handles, guns). A small amount of cyanoacrylate is placed in the bottom of a closed chamber such as jar, aquarium, or cardboard box. The object bearing the latent prints is suspended in the top of the chamber. The chamber is then sealed, and heat and moisture are introduced. For example, a light bulb or warming plate and a cup of water are adequate. Over time, a reaction occurs causing the cyanoacrylate to change from a liquid to a gas (sublimation). It is attracted to any body oil residue present in the chamber.

Unlike iodine fumigation where results are often ready within a few minutes, visible prints from cyanoacrylate fumigation can take from one to 24 hours or more. After a reaction does occur, the object is removed and is analyzed and/or photographed. An advantage to prints enhanced by cyanoacrylate is that they do not degrade. They are permanent records.
