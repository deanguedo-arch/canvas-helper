Interpreting DNA Match Probability Ratios

To understand better the meaning of the population frequency values presented with DNA profiles, consider these two examples.

Example #1: Weak DNA Match

DNA from unknown blood at a crime scene was matched to the DNA from the prime suspect. In addition to the forensic experts declaring a match, the DNA sample is compared to applicable population frequency data. The numerical chance that the DNA match could belong to someone other than the suspect was calculated to be 1 in 8 900

This means that one out of every 8 900 people could also match the DNA from the crime scene.

Example #2: Strong DNA Match

DNA from unknown blood at a crime scene was matched to the DNA from the prime suspect. In addition to the forensic experts declaring a match, the DNA sample is compared to applicable population frequency data. The numerical chance that the DNA match could belong to someone other than the suspect was calculated to be: 1 in 2 000 000

This means that one out of every two million people could also match the DNA from the crime scene.

Conclusion:

The probability ratio in Example #2 is a much stronger match than Example #1. Example #2 is a more convincing piece of DNA evidence because of the significantly lower chance that the DNA matches another person’s. The reason that fewer people in the general population match the DNA in Example #2 is that the VNTR/STR sequence that was analyzed was an unusual pattern in human DNA.
