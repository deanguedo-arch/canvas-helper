The Use of DNA Population Frequency Values in Court 


 

Probability ratios based on population frequency values have been known to confuse jurors and even some judges during criminal trials. This is because the mathematics behind these ratios is not easily understood. This confusion can lead to incorrect verdicts. Frequently in criminal trials involving DNA evidence, experts are brought in not only to explain the results but also to explain how the results were determined.

Defense lawyers are quite aware that DNA evidence can be difficult to understand; therefore, they do their best to play up this fact. Lawyers for the accused often try to convince jurors that the mere existence of a chance of a DNA sample matching someone else in the population is enough to “prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt”.

To eliminate the doubt in DNA evidence that some may have, the public must be educated about what DNA is and how DNA profiles and matches are determined. Two effective ways to educate people about forensic DNA profiling are by increasing the incorporation of this scientific concept in school curriculums and by publicizing in the mass media criminal cases that use DNA evidence .

Canada's National DNA Bank



In July 2000, after years of lobbying by police and Crown prosecutors across Canada, a federal law was passed by Parliament that established a national criminal DNA data bank. The law was not passed without controversy; defense lawyers portrayed the law as an invasion of privacy.

Canada’s national DNA data bank stores DNA collected from unsolved crimes committed across the country, and it stores DNA from individuals convicted of serious crimes. The crime(s) of which one must be for DNA to be taken include the following:

  • manslaughter
  • murder
  • aggravated assault
  • assault with a weapon
  • violent sex crimes
  • impaired driving causing death
  • robbery
  • possession of child pornography
  • break and enter
  • assaulting a police officer

 

After a convicted criminal’s DNA record is entered into the national DNA data bank, it is compared to samples found at various unsolved crimes. If there is a DNA match, police are notified and the case is reopened.

Many police agencies believe that the establishment of a national DNA data bank is a breakthrough crime fighting tool. Some agencies believe that the law is not tough enough and want DNA samples obtained from every convicted criminal and not just the those who commit serious crimes. They think this would establish a comprehensive data bank that could provide better results.

The establishment of Canada’s national DNA data bank has lead to some impressive results. Mroe than 84 000 DNA samples from convicted criminals have been entered into the databank since 2000. Another 24 020 samples have been taken and stored into the data bank from crime scenes across Canada. Matches between crime scene DNA and suspect DNA have been made in 3 966 cases while another 526 samples have been matched to other crime scenes.

Julian Fantino, the chief of police from Toronto, Ontario, has suggested that Canada is lagging behind other countries with regard to the forensic use of DNA as an investigative tool. Chief Fantino would like to see police take DNA samples from any person who has been arrested.

Source: “Do more with DNA: Fantino,” The Toronto Star, October 19, 2004.


Christopher Hampton has been brought to justice after his daughter's DNA was put on the national database following a domestic incident with her partner. Police first collected DNA samples from St Stephen's Court in Bath, Somerset, where Melanie Road, 17, was found in a pool of blood at 5.30am on June 9 in 1984.  A DNA profile of the killer, extracted from samples of semen from the scene, was loaded onto the national DNA database in the 1990s.  There was no match from the database at that time as Hampton had not been arrested for any criminal offences. 

Scientific advances meant that familial DNA links could be identified, a breakthrough that proved crucial in solving Melanie's murder. Scientific work continued and Hampton's DNA was found on four other areas of semen staining on Melanie's trousers and pants. His DNA also matched semen on swabs taken from Melanie's body during a post-mortem examination. It was also in blood spots found near Melanie's body.

Speaking outside court, Detective Chief Inspector Julie MacKay said: "The key to solving this case has been a combination of traditional police enquiries, advances in forensic science and the tenacity of a small group of officers and police staff.  Ms MacKay told reporters she believed Hampton would have been eventually caught if it had not been for his daughter's DNA being put on the national database. She praised the efforts of officers who worked on the 1984 investigation and said the senior investigating officer had died regretting Melanie's murder was unsolved.

"If in 1984 they hadn't swabbed every one of those 71 blood spots, if they hadn't meticulously examined everything that was available to them from Melanie's clothing and her body we would not have had that DNA available to us," she said.
