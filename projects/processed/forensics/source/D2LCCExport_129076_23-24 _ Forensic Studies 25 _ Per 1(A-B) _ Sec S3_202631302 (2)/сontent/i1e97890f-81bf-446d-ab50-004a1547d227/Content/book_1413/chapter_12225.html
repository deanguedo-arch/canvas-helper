Human Hair

Hair from various parts of your body differs in many ways.  Within each individual, the hair upon his head, arms, legs, face, armpits and genitalia differs in terms of structure, texture, length and color.  Hair samples from a suspect must therefore e taken from all areas of their body for an accurate comparison.  At least 10-12 hairs from each possible locale should be taken for comparison purposes.

Hair that has been colored or highlighted can be very useful as it can be clearly identified under the microscope since there will be little variation in the medulla region (middle of hair strand) of each hair.  The roots of each hair will often vary in appearance from the rest of the hair strand.  Untreated hair tends to have a darker-colored medulla and a lighter cortex (outside).  However, keep in mind that coloring one's hair has become progressively more common, though more popular among women than men.  One of the best types of dyed hair evidence involves hair that is a very unusual color such as blue, green or orange, making a suspect easier to identify.


Various strands of color-treated hair

The length of a hair sample can be very helpful, especially if it is a long strand.  Long strands (greater than 5 cm) of human hair can only come from two places on the body - most likely the head or from a long beard.  The length of the hair may help you pinpoint the gender of the suspect, however once again both males and females can have long hair.  It is somewhat more common in Western society for males to wear their hair short, however this is not always the case.  

A hair sample that is less than 1 cm in length likely came from the arms, legs, eyebrow/eyelash or genitalia.  Hairs from these areas all appear short in length and under the microscope they are tapered in appearance (wider at one end, thinner/pointed at the other end).  Hair from the genitalia (also called pubic hair) tends to be very curly, thick in diameter and is longer than hair found on arms, legs & eyebrows.
