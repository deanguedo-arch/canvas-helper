Part A: The Nature of Psychology

One hundred years ago, the word psychology was strange and new. The source explains that the idea moved from the study of the soul, to the study of the mind, then to the study of consciousness, and finally to the broader modern meaning: the science of human behaviour, including mental and physical processes that affect it.

Why psychology caught people's attention

The lesson does not begin with a definition list. It begins with curiosity. People wanted to know why dreams could feel so real, why impossible things seemed to happen in sleep, and what, if anything, left the body when a person died. The subject became popular because it deals with the questions people naturally ask about themselves.

That is why the course keeps returning to the idea that psychology is the study of ourselves. It is not just about therapy, illness, or labels. It is about trying to describe and understand the behaviour, thoughts, feelings, and body processes that shape human life.

The source also shows that psychology did not arrive fully formed. People first used ideas like soul and mind, then narrowed the field to consciousness, and only later recognized that much behaviour and thinking happens outside full awareness. That is why the modern definition becomes broader rather than narrower.

How the meaning changed over time

In the older view, psychology was tied to the Greek idea of the psyche, meaning mind or soul. Later, psychologists treated the mind as if it were separate from the body. Then the field narrowed further into the study of consciousness, which meant what a person is aware of through sensations, thoughts, pain, colour, smell, or shape.

That older definition eventually became too small. The course explains that a good deal of behaviour and even some thinking take place below the conscious level. Once that was recognized, psychology had to expand again.

That is the important Unit 1 shift: psychology becomes the study of all behaviour, whether conscious or unconscious, and whether it is shaped mainly by thought, bodily processes, social conditions, or learning from the environment.

Psychologist vs. Psychiatrist

A common point of confusion is the difference between these two professions. The key distinction is medical training. Psychiatrists have medical degrees and can prescribe medication to their clients, whereas psychologists typically hold a Ph.D. or Psy.D. and focus on psychotherapy and testing.

The Working Goals of Psychology

1. Observe

To carefully observe and record facts about human behaviour using empirical evidence.

2. Explain

To explain the collected data and figure out why the behaviour happens.

3. Predict

To make reasonable predictions about future behaviour based on a tested hypothesis.

4. Gain Control

To use knowledge of behaviour to improve choices, solve problems, and avoid becoming helpless "victims of fate."

Description comes first

The source is strict about the first goal of psychology: observe and record facts accurately. That sounds simple until real behaviour gets involved. Human behaviour is complicated, and two people can watch the same event and tell different stories about what happened.

That is why the lesson insists on separating observation from inference. Observation reports what can actually be seen or heard. Inference adds an interpretation about what the person must have been feeling or meaning. Psychology needs both, but it has to label them honestly.

Prediction is useful, not magical

The third goal of psychology is prediction, but the textbook does not pretend prediction is easy. A hypothesis is only a well-informed starting point, not a guarantee. Predictions improve when observations are careful and when the theory being used actually fits the behaviour.

The lesson is honest about the limit: even strong psychologists do not have human behaviour completely figured out. Unit 1 keeps prediction tied to evidence and theory, not to fortune-telling or exaggerated certainty.

Observation vs inference: the unit says psychologists must separate what was actually seen from what is being interpreted. "The person smiled and laughed" is observation. "The person felt overwhelming happiness" is inference.

Professional Pathways in Psychology

Unit 1 expects students to distinguish between the helpers in the field, not just recognize the job titles.

Psychiatrist

A physician who specializes in mental illness and abnormal behaviour.

  • Education: medical degree plus postgraduate specialization
  • Main work: diagnosis, psychotherapy, medical treatment, consultation
  • Key difference: can prescribe medication

Clinical Psychologist

A psychology specialist focused on emotional and behaviour problems.

  • Education: advanced graduate work in psychology
  • Main work: testing, assessment, therapy, research
  • Key difference: not a medical doctor

Family Counsellor

A counselling professional who helps families solve problems before they become crises.

  • Education: bachelor-level background plus postgraduate counselling preparation
  • Main work: prevention, consultation, outreach, vocational support, short-term therapy
  • Key difference: does not prescribe medication

Paraprofessionals: the field also includes helpers who support psychological services without being physicians. Unit 1 treats this as a reminder that not everyone working near mental health has a medical degree.

Part B: Psychological Research

Psychology relies on empirical evidence - data that is carefully collected from a controlled experiment or observation, rather than just guessing or relying on common sense. When conducting experiments, researchers use variables.

Independent Variable

The manipulated variable. This is the factor that the researcher intentionally changes or controls to see what effect it has.

Dependent Variable

The responding variable. This is what the researcher measures. It "depends" on what was changed in the independent variable.

Case Study: Pain & Hair Colour

Researcher Dr. Sessler hypothesized that people with red hair feel pain more intensely. He enlisted ten women with dark brown hair and ten with red hair, gave them a common anaesthetic, and administered a mild electric shock, adjusting the anaesthetic until the twitching reflex stopped. He found redheads needed 20% more anaesthetic.

  • Independent Variable: Hair colour (Red vs. Brown).
  • Dependent Variable: Amount of anaesthetic needed.

Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a prediction. It comes before the experiment and states what the researcher thinks will happen.

Example: "The expensive shampoo will clean the best."

Controls and Extraneous Factors

Controls keep important conditions the same, while extraneous factors are unwanted influences that can distort the results.

Examples of controls: same wig type, same amount of shampoo, same water temperature.

Reliability and Validity

Reliability means results stay similar when the procedure is repeated. Validity means the design really measures what it claims to measure.

Repeating a study improves reliability. Measuring actual cleanliness supports validity.

Statistics matter too

Psychologists use statistics to make sense of data. Unit 1 treats this as an important caution: different statistical tests can produce different kinds of results, so data always needs careful interpretation.

Field Studies vs. Laboratory Studies

Laboratory studies happen in controlled settings, which makes variables easier to manage.

Field studies happen in natural settings, so behaviour may look more realistic. In Unit 1 they are often treated as more ethical because they interfere less with the subject's normal environment.

Neither is automatically "better." The choice depends on whether control or realism matters more.

Ethics and Animal Research

Research in psychology is not only about good design. It also has to meet ethical standards, especially when animals are involved.

  • Canadian Council on Animal Care: monitors animal research in Canada.
  • Why it matters: its rules influence what research is allowed and can affect whether institutions receive research money.
  • Big idea: good evidence is not enough by itself; psychology also asks whether the study is responsible and humane.

Part D: The Birth of a Science

For centuries, major thinkers like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle asked sharp questions about behaviour, but their ideas were mostly speculative and untested. Unit 1 treats the real turning point as the moment psychology stopped being mostly argument and became an experimental science.

The First Laboratory (1879)

German psychologist Wilhelm Wundt started the first real psychological laboratory in Leipzig, Germany in 1879. By using controlled experiments and published data, he gave psychology status and recognition as a legitimate science.

Why Wundt mattered

The course treats Wundt as the turning point because he moved psychology out of the realm of fascinating but untested ideas. Before that, even brilliant thinkers like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle were still offering speculative explanations. Wundt changed the standard by saying those ideas had to be examined through laboratory work.

That shift matters for the whole unit. When the page says psychology became a science, it does not mean the older thinkers were useless. It means the field gained recognition when it started testing, recording, and publishing evidence instead of stopping at argument.

What the first lab actually studied

Wundt was interested in how sensations and feelings shaped judgment. The source lists the kinds of things his lab investigated: sensations, feelings, attention, perception, reaction time, and association. Those topics may sound basic now, but they were crucial because they could be observed, measured, and compared.

The important takeaway is not that Wundt solved psychology. The important takeaway is that he helped define a method: build a setting where mental life can be examined in a disciplined way, then publish results so the field can move forward on evidence.

Timeline Notes You Need

1) Early philosophy and medicine: people asked what the mind is and why humans behave as they do, but there was little testing.

2) Wundt (1879): psychology becomes a lab science in Leipzig with observation, measurement, and formal experimentation.

3) Freud (late 1800s to early 1900s): psychoanalysis focuses on the unconscious, inner conflict, and early experience.

4) Behaviourism (early to mid-1900s): Watson and Skinner shift focus to observable behaviour, reinforcement, and learning.

5) Humanism (mid-1900s): Maslow and Rogers emphasize growth, choice, meaning, and self-actualization.

Key Terms for This Part

Empirical evidence: conclusions built from systematic observation and data rather than guesswork.

Introspection: early method of reporting your own conscious experiences.

Structuralism: early attempt to break conscious experience into basic parts.

Functionalism: approach focused on what behaviour and mental processes do for adaptation.

School of thought: a major framework for explaining behaviour and mental processes.

Wundt's lab topics: sensations, feelings, attention, perception, reaction time, and association.

Part E: Major Schools of Thought

As psychology grew, different thinkers developed unique ways of explaining human behaviour. These became known as the "Schools of Thought."

Why the schools disagree

Unit 1 does not present these schools as interchangeable slogans. They disagree because they start with different assumptions about what matters most. Freud pays attention to unconscious conflict and early experience. Behaviourists trust what can be directly observed and measured. Humanists focus on growth, choice, and the drive toward fulfillment.

That means each school notices something important and leaves something out. The value of this part of the unit is learning what each school sees clearly, what it may exaggerate, and why later psychologists often borrowed from more than one approach.

Psychoanalysis

Core idea: unconscious motives and early experiences shape behaviour.

Key figure: Sigmund Freud

Behaviourism

Core idea: behaviour is learned and can be shaped by environment and consequences.

Key figures: John B. Watson, B.F. Skinner

Humanism

Core idea: people can choose growth, meaning, and healthy change.

Key figures: Abraham Maslow, Carl Rogers

Eclectic View

Core idea: combine methods from several schools based on the case.

Key idea: practical integration

Sigmund Freud & The Unconscious Mind

Freud argued that the unconscious mind is much larger than the conscious mind. The lesson uses his iceberg analogy: only the small visible tip is conscious, while the far larger hidden mass represents unconscious wishes, memories, fears, and conflicts.

The unit also names a preconscious region between the two. This is material you are not thinking about right now, but may be able to bring forward with clues, concentration, or reflection.

Freud's treatment methods also matter in this unit because they show how he tried to reach material that was not easily available to awareness. Hypnosis relaxed the patient, free association let the patient talk without rigid filtering, and dream interpretation treated dreams as emotionally meaningful rather than random nonsense.

Freud's Theory of Personality

Freud used hypnosis, free association, and dream interpretation to reach unconscious material. He believed dreams were meaningful, tied to emotion, and often expressed wishes in disguised form rather than as obvious literal scenes.

He also believed the human personality was composed of three main conflicting areas. Click to reveal them:

The Superego

The moral compass or conscience. Freud says it forms around ideas of right and wrong learned from important authority figures and pushes the person toward ideal behaviour.

Carl Jung

Jung agreed the unconscious mattered, but he did not reduce behaviour only to early childhood conflict. He said goals also direct behaviour. He popularized introvert, extravert, and ambivert, and divided the unconscious into a personal unconscious and a universal collective unconscious.

Behaviourism

Behaviourists were frustrated with methods like hypnosis, dream interpretation, and long reflection on early memories. They wanted psychology to focus on what could be seen, measured, and tested directly.

The source pushes this contrast hard. A Freudian might ask what forgotten childhood conflict produced fear. A behaviourist would measure pulse, muscle tension, sweating, or the visible response to a stimulus. In other words, the behaviourist wanted psychology to behave more like a laboratory science and less like symbolic interpretation.

  • They believed it was only valid to measure outward, observable behaviour.
  • They argued that behaviour is shaped by the environment through stimulus-response connections.
  • John Watson pushed an extreme environmental position tied to tabula rasa, treating the newborn as a kind of blank slate.
  • The lesson notes Watson reduced inborn emotions to fear, rage, and love, and treated many other reactions as conditioned responses.
  • The Little Albert study is used to show the behaviourist claim that fears can be learned.

This school is also why Unit 1 later cares about overt and covert behaviour. Behaviourism strongly prefers what is overt because that is what another observer can reliably measure.

Humanism

Humanists (like Abraham Maslow and Carl Rogers) emerged as a reaction against both Psychoanalysis and Behaviourism. They felt those theories were too pessimistic or too mechanical to capture human growth.

The unit's humanistic material is more than a five-level pyramid to memorize. Maslow argues that people move upward through kinds of need in a generally orderly way, but not in a perfect mechanical sequence. A person does not need to satisfy one level 100% before caring about the next, and different people need different amounts of approval, safety, or belonging before they feel secure enough to move on.

  • They focused on human potential, free will, and the drive toward healthy growth.
  • Abraham Maslow described a hierarchy of needs: physiological needs, safety, belonging and love, esteem, and self-actualization.
  • The unit says lower needs usually must be met at least to some degree before higher needs can dominate attention.
  • Maslow describes self-actualization in rich terms like honesty, truth, beauty, individuality, humour, justice, and self-determination.
  • Carl Rogers emphasized the self-concept and argued people can form their own identity rather than only being shaped by outside expectations.

Rogers matters because he shifts attention toward the way people see themselves. The self-concept influences behaviour, and healthy change means building a self-view that is both positive and realistic.

The Eclectic View

Most modern psychologists do not strictly adhere to just one of these historical schools.

An eclectic view selects the strongest ideas from more than one approach, but the unit warns that this is not random mixing. The final theory or treatment still has to stay orderly and coherent.

That warning matters. Eclecticism is not permission to throw together every idea that sounds interesting. It means weighing evidence, choosing compatible principles, and staying open enough to revise your theory when new facts appear.

Part F: Altered States

Sleep and Dreams

The source is much fuller than a one-line summary here. Sleep is treated as a necessary biological process that helps the brain sort the events of the day, supports resistance to stress, strengthens the immune system, and protects concentration and memory.

The lesson also names the costs of sleep loss: anxiety, depression, memory trouble, poor concentration, accidents, illness, and personality disturbance.

Hypnosis

Freud used hypnosis early in his career because deep relaxation can make certain memories and wishes easier to bring forward. But Unit 1 is careful not to romanticize it.

It explicitly says not everyone can be hypnotized. Willingness, cooperation, focus, and safety matter more than treating hypnosis like a guaranteed psychological shortcut.

Sleep is not just "doing nothing"

The textbook pushes back against the lazy idea that sleep is passive dead time. It compares the brain to a system that needs to go off line so it can sort and restore itself. While we are awake, nerve impulses are constantly moving through the system. Sleep changes that pattern and gives the body a chance to restore what it needs for focused thinking.

That is why the unit connects sleep with concentration, memory, stress resistance, and immune strength. It also explains why long-term sleep loss spills into the next day as irritability, poor judgment, weak attention, illness, or accidents.

The five-stage model matters because the course does not treat sleep as one flat state. Different things happen across the cycle, and REM sleep has a special link to dreaming.

Dreams and meaning

Unit 1 keeps dreams in two frames at once. On one hand, dreams have a biological relationship to REM sleep, attention, and mental restoration. On the other hand, Freud and Jung both treated dreams as psychologically meaningful, even if they disagreed about exactly what they meant.

The source does not tell students to memorize a universal dream dictionary. Instead, it pushes them to notice patterns, emotions, repetition, and personal symbols. That is a much more careful approach than pretending every dream image has one fixed public meaning.

The practical skill here is interpretation with context: what was happening in your life, what emotions were present, and what repeated images keep returning.

Reflect: Hypnosis Reality Check

Use the notes from Part F to think through when hypnosis might help and when it might create risk.

Unqualified hypnosis can disguise serious problems and delay proper treatment. People also need focused concentration and willing cooperation, which is why very young children or some cognitively impaired people may not be good hypnosis candidates.

The key point for Unit 1 is practical: hypnosis is not universal, and safety matters more than treating it like a guaranteed technique.

Sleep stages the unit expects you to know

  • Stage 1: light sleep, drifting in and out, easy to wake.
  • Stage 2: eye movement stops and brain-wave pattern slows.
  • Stages 3 and 4: deep sleep, harder to wake, linked with night terrors, sleep walking, and strong delta waves.
  • REM: dream sleep, rapid eye movement, faster body functions, and changing amounts across the lifespan.

What to remember

  • Dreams mattered historically: Freud called them the "royal road" to the unconscious, and Jung treated dream symbols as meaningful too.
  • Sleep matters biologically: the lesson links sleep with memory sorting, stress resistance, and immune strength.
  • REM matters: dream deprivation can produce irritability, concentration trouble, and memory lapses.
  • Sleep changes in stages: Unit 1 expects you to know the five-stage pattern rather than treating sleep as one flat state.
  • Hypnosis is not universal: some people are much more responsive than others.
  • Do not oversimplify it: Unit 1 does not treat hypnotizability like a neat fixed label that automatically explains every person.
  • Cooperation is required: very young children or some cognitively impaired people may not be able to give the focused attention hypnosis requires.
  • Safety matters: a procedure is not automatically helpful just because it sounds psychological.

Part G: Overt and Covert Behaviour

Unit 1 does not only ask what psychology studies. It also asks how behaviour is described. Some behaviour can be seen directly, while some behaviour happens internally and has to be inferred.

Overt Behaviour

Overt behaviour is visible. Another person can directly observe the action as it happens.

  • Laughing at a joke
  • Raising a hand in class
  • Walking out of a room
  • Speaking, crying, or stomping

Covert Behaviour

Covert behaviour is hidden inside the person. It includes private thoughts, feelings, and mental rehearsal.

  • Worrying about a test
  • Feeling jealous without showing it
  • Mentally rehearsing a speech
  • Remembering, imagining, or silently judging

Why the distinction matters

Behaviourists

Preferred overt behaviour because it could be observed and measured directly.

Cognitive psychologists

Often study covert processes such as thinking, memory, and perception.

Assignment writing

If an action can be seen, call it overt. If it stays private inside the person, call it covert.

Part A: What Learning Is

Unit 2 defines learning as gaining the ability to respond competently to a situation. The lesson insists on a key distinction: learning is behaviour modified through experience, not just anything the body does.

Core distinction

Reflexes are not learning. A reflex is an automatic response that does not require practice or previous experience. Learning begins when a person uses curiosity, practice, and feedback to build a more competent response.

The Three Pathways of Learning

Perceptual Motor Learning

This is the learning of physical skills. The course explains that a learner must first perceive the action clearly and then train the body to carry it out accurately. Tying a bow, skiing, doing a somersault, or learning bicycle balance all fit this category.

What the learner must do

Notice the movement pattern accurately, break it into steps, and repeat the coordinated action until the body can do it smoothly.

Typical examples

Tying shoes, skating, handwriting, serving a volleyball, keyboarding, or any other skill where seeing and doing must work together.

Affective Learning

Affective learning covers feelings, values, likes, dislikes, social rules, and ways of behaving toward other people. It is how human beings learn what matters, what feels appropriate, and how to function socially with others.

What belongs here

Values, emotional responses, biases, social expectations, and the rules we learn for living with family, friends, and the wider community.

Why it matters

The course treats affective learning as a vital part of sociability because it shapes how people respond to praise, rejection, conflict, and belonging.

Cognitive Learning

Cognitive learning is the school-type learning most students immediately recognize. It includes thinking, problem solving, concept building, and processing information in creative ways.

Why Unit 2 starts here

Before the course talks about conditioning, it wants you to understand that learning can be physical, emotional, and intellectual. The rest of the unit builds from that wider definition.

Part B: Self-Concept, Motivation, and Attitude

Unit 2 makes the point that learning is not just about information. The learner brings a self-concept, a level of motivation, and a pattern of attitudes into every task. Those factors shape confidence, persistence, and the willingness to keep going when the work feels hard.

Positive self-concept

Learners expect they can improve, tolerate setbacks better, and are more willing to keep trying. The course treats this as a real advantage, not just a nice feeling.

Negative self-concept

Learners feel inferior, doubt their own ability, and may shut down early. The unit's labelling example with rats shows how expectations can change performance by changing the way others respond.

Motivation asks: "What's in it for me?"

The source says learners usually do best when they are interested, when they intend to learn, when the material feels meaningful, and when they can apply it to real life.

  • High motivation: creates energy, focus, and persistence.
  • Weak motivation: makes the task feel pointless and easy to avoid.
  • Attitude: can strengthen or sabotage learning long before a test ever happens.
Big barrier named in the unit: fear of failure, embarrassment, and rejection can stop learning before the skill has even had a real chance to form.

Motivation and support change how hard learning feels.

Part C: Learning Curve and Efficiency

How progress actually looks

The unit stresses that learning is often irregular. At first a learner may feel confused and frustrated. Then progress speeds up as the material starts making sense. Later it slows again as the learner moves toward mastery and refinement.

1. Slow start

The learner is still figuring out the rules, the vocabulary, and the basic moves.

2. Faster growth

Connections form more quickly once the learner becomes comfortable with the task.

3. Mastery and polish

Progress slows because the learner is refining, consolidating, and making performance more exact.

Plateaus, transfer, and smarter practice

A plateau is a temporary standstill in learning, not permanent failure. The unit says plateaus often come from boredom, anxiety, weak background knowledge, or poor study procedures.

Efficiency ideas named in the source:

  • Transfer: earlier learning can help or interfere with later learning.
  • Simulation: a special kind of positive transfer because practice resembles real life.
  • Overlearning: continue until the material is thoroughly mastered.
  • Distributed practice: spaced sessions usually beat one giant cram session.
Memory helpers the unit names:
serial learning, primacy and recency effects, mnemonic devices, latent learning, insight, trial and error, and natural association.

Part D: Efficient Learning Tools

The source does not say "try harder" and leave it there. It names the specific factors that help or interfere with learning: transfer, overlearning, distributed practice, mnemonic devices, latent learning, insight, natural association, intensity, and the use of advanced organizers.

When progress stalls

  • Plateau: a temporary standstill, not proof that the learner cannot succeed.
  • Common causes: fatigue, boredom, anxiety, weak background knowledge, and poor study procedures.
  • Negative transfer: earlier learning can interfere when an old response keeps getting in the way of the new one.
  • Saturation: after too much input at once, the learner cannot take in much more.

What makes learning stick

  • Distributed practice: shorter sessions with rest usually beat a long cram session.
  • Overlearning: keep practicing after the first success so the skill becomes dependable.
  • Advanced organizers: preview the big structure before diving into the details.
  • Mnemonic devices: use catchwords, patterns, or phrases to support recall.

How insight grows

  • Latent learning: the learner has taken something in even before it shows up in performance.
  • Insight: earlier pieces suddenly connect and the solution becomes clear.
  • Natural association: memory improves when ideas belong together in a meaningful way.
  • Intensity: vivid or emotionally strong experiences often last longer in memory.

Part E: Preferences, Reinforcement, and Conditioning

The final stretch of Unit 2 turns from study procedure to habit building. The source talks about visual, auditory, and kinesthetic preferences, and then explains how reinforcement and conditioning shape future responses.

Learning preferences and study systems

  • Visual learners: benefit from diagrams, layout, colour, and picture-based organization.
  • Auditory learners: often remember best through hearing, speaking, and replaying ideas out loud.
  • Kinesthetic/tactual learners: learn through movement, handling, and doing.
  • SQ3R and mind maps: the unit presents study systems that organize information before memorization begins.

Important limit: a learning preference is a planning clue, not an excuse to avoid every other mode. The goal is better learning, not self-labelling.

Reinforcement and conditioning

  • Operant conditioning: the learner does something first, then consequences change the chance of doing it again.
  • Classical conditioning: a new stimulus gets paired with a response it did not originally produce.
  • Negative reinforcement: removing something unpleasant can strengthen a behaviour.
  • Punishment: may stop behaviour briefly, but the unit warns that it does not clearly teach the better response.
  • Intermittent reinforcement: behaviour can stay strong even when reinforcement does not happen every time.

Thinking and Memory

How do we encode, store, and retrieve information? Explore the mechanics of memory, the processes of human thought, and the true meaning of intelligence.

Study flow: Learn the section content first, complete the embedded activity right after that section, then finish the unit knowledge check at the end. All required answer information is inside this module page.

Part A: Thought & Problem Solving

Thought and language are two tools used every minute of the waking day. Our advanced form of thinking sets us apart from all other animals.

Directed Thinking

Systematic and logical thinking. It has a specific goal, like solving a math problem or planning a trip.

Undirected Thinking

A free flow of thoughts, such as daydreaming. It has no particular goal or structured plan.

Problem Solving Strategies

When faced with an obstacle, we use different cognitive strategies to find a solution:

Trial and Error: Trying various solutions one by one until the correct one is found.
Insight: The sudden realization of a solution. The "Aha!" moment where the answer suddenly becomes clear.

Part B: The Mechanics of Memory

Memory is the capacity to encode, store, and retrieve information. One specific type is verbal memory, which involves abstract data related to thoughts, words, and ideas.

The Zeigarnik Effect

Have you ever noticed that you can easily remember the details of a TV show that ended on a cliffhanger, but quickly forget the plot of a movie that resolved perfectly?

The Zeigarnik Effect is the psychological theory that incomplete tasks are remembered longer and more vividly than completed tasks.

Part C: Defining Intelligence

Determining intelligence strictly on IQ scores has become widely criticized. Modern psychology views intelligence as a complex, multi-faceted concept rather than a single number.

Raymond Cattell's Two Types of Intelligence

Fluid Intelligence

Often referred to as "Street Smarts." This is the type of intelligence a person is born with. It involves the ability to reason quickly, think abstractly, and adapt to new situations without relying on prior learning.

Crystallized Intelligence

Often referred to as "Book Smarts." This is the intelligence learned in school. It relies on the accumulation of knowledge, facts, and skills acquired through experience and education over a lifetime.

Howard Gardner's Multiple Intelligences

Gardner proposed that people do not have just an intellectual capacity, but have many kinds of intelligence. A person might struggle with math, but be a genius in music or bodily movement.

IQ Testing & Bias

You can study for an intelligence test, but there are many external factors that can negatively affect a person's results, making the score inaccurate. True bias has not been entirely eliminated from IQ tests.

Roles and Group Influences

An introduction to Social Psychology. Discover how the presence of others affects our attitudes, the roles we play, and how our birth order shapes our personality.

Study flow: Learn the section content first, complete the embedded activity right after that section, then finish the unit knowledge check at the end. All required answer information is inside this module page.

Part A: The Roles We Play

"All the world's a stage,
And all the men and women merely players;
They have their exits and their entrances;
And one man in his time plays many parts."
- William Shakespeare

In psychology, a role is defined as the behaviour pattern one assumes in a given situation. Just like an actor on a stage, you behave differently depending on the "scene" you are in and the "audience" watching you.

Role Dynamics

Because we play many parts (student, employee, sibling, friend), we sometimes experience Role Conflict. This happens when the demands of one role clash directly with the demands of another.

Example: You are a dedicated student who needs to study for a final exam tonight, but you are also an employee whose boss just called demanding you cover a shift.

Society often dictates strict "gender roles" for how men and women should behave. Androgyny refers to a person who blends these roles, exhibiting both traditionally masculine and feminine characteristics, allowing for greater behavioral flexibility.

Part B: Birth Order Influences

Many psychologists believe that the position you occupy in your family's birth order has a significant influence on the development of your personality. While there are always exceptions, researchers have identified common traits associated with birth order.

Part C: The Influence of Groups

Human beings are social animals. Groups of one kind or another play a tremendous part in shaping our attitudes, our thoughts, and ultimately our behaviour.

Classifying Groups

Task-Oriented Group

A group whose primary aim is to perform a specific job or achieve a specific goal. (Example: A school project committee, a surgical team, or a jury).

Social Group

A group formed primarily for personal relationships, enjoyment, and social interaction rather than completing a strict task. (Example: A group of friends going to the movies).

When Groups Turn Negative

While groups provide support, they also create boundaries of "Us" versus "Them". This can lead to severe psychological and social issues.

  • Xenophobia: An irrational fear, intense dislike, or prejudice against people from other countries, cultures, or outsiders in general. It is rooted in the fear of the "out-group."
  • Bullying & Exclusion: Being pushed outside of the group. The psychological pain of exclusion (such as unemployment or severe bullying) triggers the same neural pathways in the brain as physical pain.

The Need to Belong

Our most fundamental group is often our family, but as we develop, our peer groups become increasingly influential. Peer pressure can force individuals to conform to group norms, changing their natural behaviour simply to fit in.

Despite the negatives, close friendships that last a lifetime are the strongest form of group belonging. Building a better you often involves creating meaningful, positive relationships through effective communication and empathy.

Emotions & Criminal Behaviour

Analyze the complex nature of human emotion and examine the psychological factors, mental disorders, and environmental influences that contribute to criminal behaviour.

Study flow: Learn the section content first, complete the embedded activity right after that section, then finish the unit knowledge check at the end. All required answer information is inside this module page.

Part A: The Roots of Behaviour

What causes criminal behaviour? Why might one member of a family be criminally deviant while the rest are law-abiding citizens? Psychologists frame this question around one of the oldest debates in science: Nature vs. Nurture.

Nature

Refers to the influence of genetics and biology. Some psychologists believe that certain criminal tendencies (like psychopathy) are innate - that some people are simply born with a predisposition to deviance.

Nurture

Refers to the influence of the environment. This perspective argues that criminal behavior develops due to poor socialization in early childhood, alienation from parents, or learning from deviant peer groups.

Part B: Psychological Profiles

Criminal behavior comes in many forms, driven by different underlying psychological motives ranging from a complete lack of empathy to uncontrollable urges.

The Psychopathic Personality

A psychopath may initially seem charming and highly intelligent, yet they possess deeply dangerous qualities. They are habitual liars, highly self-centred, impulsive, and emotionally callous.

A Missing Superego

In Freudian terms, a psychopath is a person with no conscience (no superego). Therefore, they have absolutely no moral principles guiding their relationships with others, allowing them to manipulate and harm without guilt.

Origins:

The psychopathic personality may develop due to poor socialization in early childhood or alienation from close bonds with parents. However, some psychologists argue that psychopaths are simply born that way (nature).

Compulsion and Urges

Sometimes, crimes are committed not for financial gain or strategy, but due to deep-seated, uncontrollable psychological urges.

Pyromania

The uncontrollable urge to set fires. Psychologically, this is often caused by a deep rebellion against authority, destructive urges, and occasionally the quest for sexual excitement.

Kleptomania

An uncontrolled urge to steal items, even when the items are unneeded or inexpensive. Actions may be based on sexual excitement or motivated by revenge against authority figures.

Assault and Cult Dynamics

Sexual Assault

Psychologically, sexual assault is less about sexual gratification and primarily about sexual control and violation of someone against their will. The rapist seeks to express deep-seated anger, rage, and dominance over their victim.

Cults

Cults are groups that aggressively recruit and meticulously control people. They operate based on the unique perspective of the cult leader, which could be disguised as religion, politics, self-discovery, or even marketing motives. They utilize extreme psychological manipulation and isolate members from their outside support systems.

Youth Crime

The justice system recognizes that the developing brain of an adolescent requires a different approach than that of a fully matured adult.

The Young Offenders' Act (1984)

This Canadian legislation was passed to deal specifically with young people between 12 and 18 years of age.

  • It advocates for shorter prison terms for youth crime compared to adults.
  • It strictly protects the identity of these young people to prevent them from being permanently branded as criminals early in life.

Part C: A Dark History - Eugenics in Alberta

Psychology and psychiatry have not always been used ethically. One of the darkest chapters in Canadian psychological history occurred right here in Alberta, driven by the pseudo-science of Eugenics.

Established in 1928, this legislation allowed the Alberta Eugenics Board to authorize the sterilization of mentally disabled and socially vulnerable people. The stated goal was to prevent the transmission of "undesirable traits" to offspring. (British Columbia also had similar legislation).

Targeted Groups Included:

Females, children, rural citizens, the unemployed, domestic help, unmarried people, those in mental institutions, Catholics, those of Ukrainian and First Nation ancestry, individuals with lower intelligence, alcoholics, and people with epilepsy.

Leilani Muir was a woman of completely normal intelligence who was wrongfully sterilized under this Act. As an unwanted and abused child, she was admitted to a school for "mental defectives" in Red Deer at the age of 10. Without her consent or understanding, she was sterilized.

In the 1990s, she successfully sued the Alberta government for this horrific violation of her human rights and was awarded approximately $740,000 plus legal costs, bringing widespread attention to the abuses of the Eugenics Board.

Occasional worry, suspicion, or anxiety is a completely normal part of the human experience. However, when these feelings become excessive, paralyzing, or prevent a person from functioning in everyday life, they cross into the territory of Neurosis.

The term "neurosis" sounds mysterious, but it is incredibly common. The primary underlying cause of neurosis is excessive anxiety.

Part C: Diagnosis & Treatment

Therapies for Phobias

Psychologists generally use two distinct behavioural approaches when helping a patient overcome a crippling phobia.

Desensitization

A gradual method where the patient confronts the feared object in small steps. Over time, the situations become more frightening, allowing the patient to slowly build tolerance while practicing relaxation techniques.

Flooding

A highly intense method where the patient is immediately forced to confront the feared object right from the beginning, without any gradual buildup. The goal is to prove that no actual harm will occur, exhausting the fear response.

The Reality of Depression

Depression is not simply "feeling sad." It is a serious physical and emotional state that causes a person to be entirely unable to function when fulfilling everyday duties.

Common Characteristics & Symptoms:
  • Deep feelings of worthlessness, hopelessness, and sadness.
  • Severe problems with sleeping (insomnia or oversleeping).
  • Restlessness and chronic fatigue.
  • Physical ailments such as digestive problems and unexplained body pains.
  • A complete inability to concentrate on tasks.
Depression requires professional medical and psychological support; it is not something a person can just "snap out of."

The Psychology of Brainwashing

In previous units, we discussed Cults. A primary tool of dangerous cults and radical extremist groups is Brainwashing.

Brainwashing involves forcibly controlling a person's mind through a strict two-step process:

  1. Destruction: Systematically breaking down and destroying the individual's current personality, self-esteem, and core beliefs through isolation, sleep deprivation, and psychological abuse.
  2. Reprogramming: Once the individual is broken and desperate for approval, they are programmed with new, radical ideas designed entirely to serve the controller or the group.

Assignment #7 | Section 3

Psychosis

A deep dive into severe mental disorders characterized by a disconnection from reality, including Schizophrenia, Bipolar Disorder, and the mechanics of human perception.

Study flow: Learn the section content first, complete the embedded activity right after that section, then finish the unit knowledge check at the end. All required answer information is inside this module page.

Part A: The Break from Reality

While neurosis involves excessive anxiety but leaves the person grounded in reality, Psychosis is described as a serious pathological state resulting in a complete loss of contact with reality.

The Psychotic Experience

Psychotics experience periods where they are unable to distinguish between what is real and what is not. Their thought patterns and emotions become highly distorted.

Misconception: A common misconception is that all psychotic individuals are dangerous, violent, or "crazy." In truth, most are simply confused, frightened, and suffer from distorted interpretations of their environment.

Part B: Forms of Psychosis

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia

The most profound psychotic disorder. Symptoms include Delusions (false beliefs, like paranoia) and Hallucinations (false sensory experiences, like hearing voices). There is strong evidence linking this to chemical imbalances in the brain.

Manic-Depression (Bipolar)

Click to reveal

Manic-Depression

Characterized by extreme mood swings. The person alternates between wild, hyperactive, euphoric periods (Mania) and deep, paralyzing lows (Depression).

Dissociative Identity

A serious fragmentation of the mind. Typically stems from severe childhood trauma, causing a person to dissociate and fragment their identity into multiple distinct personalities to protect themselves.

Organic Psychosis

Organic Psychosis

Psychosis caused by physical damage or degeneration of brain tissue. Examples include Alzheimer's Disease (degeneration affecting memory and judgment) or prolonged alcohol abuse.

Part C: Treatment Approaches

Medical Interventions

  • Drug Therapy

    The most common modern treatment for severe psychosis. Antipsychotic drugs have proven highly successful in reducing hallucinations and stabilizing mood swings (e.g., Lithium for Bipolar).

  • Electroconvulsive Shock Therapy (ECT)

    A treatment that sends a controlled electrical current through the brain to induce a brief seizure. While it has adversaries, it remains a highly effective treatment for severe, treatment-resistant depression.

Psychotherapy

  • Psychoanalysis

    Utilizes the theories of Sigmund Freud to study psychosis by deeply examining a patient's childhood experiences and unconscious mind.

  • Play Therapy

    Used specifically for behavioral and emotional disorders affecting young children who do not yet have the vocabulary to express their complex feelings.

The Eclectic Approach

No single therapy works for everyone. The Eclectic Approach involves taking the best parts of a number of different theories and medical treatments to build a customized treatment plan for an individual patient.

Part D: Sensation & Perception (The Decibel Scale)

Sensation is the physical process of receiving stimuli from the environment using one of the five senses (e.g., sound waves hitting the eardrum).

Perception is the psychological process where the brain organizes and interprets that sensory information (e.g., recognizing those sound waves as your favorite song).

Hearing Loss & Decibels (dB)

Exposure to noise is measured in decibels. Prolonged exposure to high decibels can cause permanent hearing loss and Tinnitus (a constant ringing or buzzing in the ears).
