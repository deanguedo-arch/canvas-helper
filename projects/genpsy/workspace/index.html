The Foundation & The Brain

A deep dive into the biological roots of behaviour, thought, and emotion. Discover the microscopic building blocks and the macro-structures that make you who you are.

Introduction: The Biological Basis of Behavior

Every thought you have, every emotion you feel, and every action you take is fundamentally a biological event. To truly understand psychology, we must look beneath the surface of behavior and examine the biological machinery that drives it. We begin our journey at the microscopic level with the neuron, before zooming out to the complex circuitry of the nervous system and the breathtaking architecture of the human brain.

Key Concept: Monism vs. Dualism

Historically, philosophers debated whether the mind and body were separate (Dualism) or the same (Monism). Modern biopsychology operates on the principle that "the mind is what the brain does."

Part 1: The Neuron - The Building Block

The brain contains roughly 86 billion interconnected neurons. These highly specialized cells are designed to receive, process, and transmit information across the body using electrical and chemical signals.

Anatomy of a Neuron

  • Dendrites: Branch-like extensions that receive messages from other cells. (Think of them as the "inbox").
  • Soma (Cell Body): The cell's life support center, containing the nucleus and genetic material.
  • Axon: A long, cable-like structure that passes messages away from the cell body to other neurons, muscles, or glands.
  • Myelin Sheath: A fatty layer of tissue that insulates the axon and vastly speeds up neural impulses. (Multiple Sclerosis is caused by the degradation of this sheath).
  • Terminal Buttons: The branched endings of the axon that transmit messages to other neurons via neurotransmitters.

The Action Potential: How Neurons "Fire"

Neurons communicate via an electrical charge called an Action Potential. This process follows the All-or-None Law: a neuron either fires completely or it does not fire at all—there is no "partial" firing.

  1. Resting Potential: The neuron is negatively charged inside (-70mV) and positively charged outside. It is polarized and ready to fire.
  2. Depolarization: If a threshold is reached, ion channels open. Positive sodium ions rush in, causing the inside to become temporarily positive (+40mV).
  3. Repolarization: Potassium ions flow out to restore the negative charge inside the cell.
  4. Refractory Period: A brief resting pause where the neuron pumps sodium back out and cannot fire again until the balance is restored.

The Synapse & Neurotransmitters

Neurons don't actually touch. They are separated by a microscopic gap called the synaptic cleft. When an action potential reaches the terminal buttons, it triggers the release of chemical messengers called Neurotransmitters. These chemicals cross the gap and bind to receptor sites on the receiving neuron like a key fitting into a lock.

Part 2: The Nervous System Architecture

Neurons organize into vast communication networks constituting the nervous system. The nervous system is divided into major subsystems to handle specific types of information.

The CNS consists of the Brain and the Spinal Cord. It is the "command center" of the body.

  • Spinal Cord: A two-way information highway connecting the peripheral nervous system and the brain. It is responsible for simple automatic responses to stimuli known as reflexes (like pulling your hand away from a hot stove before your brain even registers the pain).
  • Brain: The computing center responsible for our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.

The PNS gathers information and transmits CNS decisions to other body parts. It links the CNS to the body's sense receptors, muscles, and glands. It has two main components:

  • Somatic Nervous System: Controls voluntary movements of skeletal muscles. (E.g., Raising your hand).
  • Autonomic Nervous System (ANS): Controls self-regulated action of internal organs and glands. (E.g., Heartbeat, digestion).

The Autonomic Nervous System is further divided into two opposing forces that work together to maintain bodily homeostasis:

Sympathetic Nervous System

"Fight or Flight". Arouses the body, mobilizing its energy in stressful situations. Accelerates heartbeat, raises blood pressure, slows digestion, raises blood sugar, and cools with perspiration.

Parasympathetic Nervous System

"Rest and Digest". Calms the body, conserving its energy. Decreases heartbeat, lowers blood sugar, and stimulates digestion. It brings you back to a steady state after a threat has passed.

Part 3: Tour of the Brain

The brain is not just one giant mass; it is composed of highly specialized regions that evolved over millions of years. We can generally divide the brain into the primitive "Older Brain" structures, the emotional "Limbic System," and the advanced "Cerebral Cortex."

The Older Brain Structures

Medulla: Base of the brainstem. Controls heartbeat and breathing. Destroy this, and you die instantly.
Cerebellum: The "little brain" at the rear. Coordinates voluntary movement and balance. Also involved in non-verbal learning and memory.
Reticular Formation: A nerve network travelling through the brainstem. Plays an important role in controlling arousal and alertness.

The Limbic System (Emotion & Memory)

Amygdala: Two lima-bean-sized neural clusters. Linked strictly to emotion, specifically fear and aggression.
Hippocampus: A seahorse-shaped structure critical for processing conscious, explicit memories (like facts and events) for storage.
Hypothalamus: Below the thalamus. Directs maintenance activities (eating, drinking, body temperature), helps govern the endocrine system via the pituitary gland, and is linked to emotion and reward.

The Cerebral Cortex: The 4 Lobes

The ultimate control and information-processing center. Select a lobe below to explore its functions.

Frontal Lobe

Located right behind your forehead. This is the "CEO" of the brain, responsible for executive functions, decision-making, planning, judgment, and personality.

  • Motor Cortex: Located at the rear of the frontal lobes, controls voluntary movements. The right hemisphere controls the left side of the body, and vice versa.
  • Broca's Area: (Usually in the left hemisphere) controls language expression and muscle movements involved in speech.
Case Study: Phineas Gage (1848). A railroad worker who had a tamping iron blown through his left frontal lobe. He miraculously survived, but his personality drastically changed from mild-mannered to irritable and impulsive, proving the frontal lobe's role in personality and inhibition.

Parietal Lobe

Located at the top and to the rear of the head. It receives sensory input for touch and body position.

  • Somatosensory Cortex: Located at the front of the parietal lobes (right behind the motor cortex). It registers and processes body touch and movement sensations.
  • The more sensitive the body region (like the lips or fingertips), the larger the somatosensory cortex area devoted to it.
  • Also helps with mathematical and spatial reasoning.

Temporal Lobe

Located roughly above the ears. Includes the auditory areas, each receiving information primarily from the opposite ear.

  • Auditory Cortex: Processes sound.
  • Wernicke's Area: (Usually in the left temporal lobe) controls language reception—a brain area involved in language comprehension and expression. Damage here leaves a person able to speak, but the words make no sense (Word Salad).
  • Heavily involved in face recognition. Damage to specific areas can result in prosopagnosia (face blindness).

Occipital Lobe

Located at the very back of the head. This lobe is almost exclusively dedicated to vision.

  • Visual Cortex: Receives, integrates, and processes visual information from the retinas.
  • Visual information from the right half of your visual field goes to the left occipital lobe, and vice versa.
  • A blow to the back of the head can stimulate the visual cortex, causing you to "see stars."

The Divided Brain

The brain is divided into two hemispheres (Left and Right), connected by a wide band of axon fibers called the Corpus Callosum.

Left Hemisphere

Typically the "logical" side. It excels at making quick, exact interpretations of language. It handles speech production (Broca), language comprehension (Wernicke), mathematical calculation, and literal, linear thinking.

Right Hemisphere

Typically the "creative" side. Excels at making inferences, modulating speech to make meaning clear (inflection), recognizing faces, perceiving emotion, and spatial reasoning.

Note: In severe cases of epilepsy, surgeons sometimes cut the Corpus Callosum, creating "Split-Brain" patients. These fascinating case studies reveal how the two sides of the brain operate independently when they can no longer communicate.

Part 4: High-Yield Study Builder

Use this section to move from recognition-level understanding to exam-ready mastery. Focus on explaining each concept in your own words, then tie it to a behavior or symptom.

Learning Targets

  • Trace a signal from dendrite to synapse.
  • Compare CNS, PNS, somatic, and autonomic systems.
  • Map major brain regions to behavior.
  • Use lobe functions to explain injuries or deficits.

Study Route

  1. Master neuron structure and action potential first.
  2. Add neurotransmitter roles and imbalance effects.
  3. Layer in systems architecture (CNS/PNS/ANS).
  4. Finish with lobes + hemispheres + case interpretation.

Common Misses

  • Mixing up axon and dendrite functions.
  • Confusing sympathetic and parasympathetic responses.
  • Treating lobes as isolated rather than networked.
  • Forgetting that corpus callosum enables hemisphere communication.

Part 5: Applied Case Practice

These quick cases train your ability to explain behavior through nervous-system and brain-function concepts. Try to answer before opening each explanation.

Case A: A student can hear speech clearly but cannot understand word meaning.

Most likely issue: language comprehension pathways in the left temporal region (Wernicke-related processing). Hearing can remain intact while meaning extraction fails.

Case B: A person remembers childhood stories but cannot store new day-to-day events after injury.

Most likely issue: hippocampal memory encoding disruption. Older consolidated memories may persist while new explicit memory formation is impaired.

Case C: During sudden danger, pulse and breathing spike, digestion slows, and focus narrows.

System in control: sympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system. This is an adaptive mobilization pattern for immediate threat response.

Case D: A learner knows definitions but freezes when asked to connect concepts to real behavior.

Fix strategy: move from term recall to structure-function claims (for example, "If this region supports X, then injury should affect Y behavior"). Practice with short case explanations.
