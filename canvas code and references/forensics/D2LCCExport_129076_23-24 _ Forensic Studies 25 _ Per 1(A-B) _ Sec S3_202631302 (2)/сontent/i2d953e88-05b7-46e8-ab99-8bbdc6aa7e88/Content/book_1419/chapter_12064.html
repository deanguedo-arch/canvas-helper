Crime Case Studies Involving Forensic Genetics

Although DNA print analysis is a new branch of forensics, it has quickly become one of the most effective and powerful tools that can be used by forensic scientists. In crime scene investigations the analysis of nuclear DNA has proven useful in the identification of victims and the individualization of criminals. The analysis of mtDNA has been used successfully in the identification of unrecognizable human remains of combat soldiers and victims of crime, war, and horrific disasters.

The following lesson focuses upon two crime case studies that involve forensic genetics: one true crime story, The Punky Gustavson Story, and one fictional crime story, The Bandana Bank Robbery.

Crime Case Study #1: The Punky Gustavson Story

The Crime

Clifford Sleigh, 42, from Lodgepole, Alberta, went to Edmonton, Alberta, for a few days in 1992 to play in a softball tournament and visit his family. On a Friday night after drinking alcohol heavily, Clifford got into an argument and a fistfight with his brother-in-law. The next morning he got into a verbal spat with his wife. Frustrated and angry about both incidents, Clifford took off in his brother-in-law's car the morning of September 6th. During this time he bought some alcohol, got drunk, and pulled into a parking lot near a condominium complex close to where he had been staying. He spotted two young girls playing in a fenced yard nearby. After watching them for a time and not noticing any adults in the vicinity, he went to the yard and grabbed the little girl closest to the fence. Likely because she was so startled, the frightened young girl did not put up much resistance when she was abducted. Clifford Sleigh then drove the girl to an undisclosed location, sexually assaulted her, smothered her, and then dumped her lifeless body.

 The Victim and the Initial Search

The little girl abducted, sexually assaulted, and killed by Clifford Sleigh was 6-year-old Corinne "Punky" Gustavson. Corinne was given the nickname of "Punky" by her family because her blond hair had the tendency to be spiky and unruly. Punky lived with her parents in a condominium in northeast Edmonton. Her father was home on the morning she was abducted; he had been watching the girls but went inside for a few minutes. After Punky was taken, the 5-year-old friend she had been playing with ran inside to tell her mother and Punky's father. They began searching and soon contacted the Edmonton Police Service (EPS). An intense search of the surrounding area by police and volunteers yielded nothing.

Two days after the abduction, Punky was found face down in a trucking yard on the outskirts of Edmonton. The 6-year-old was fully clothed, her jacket was twisted, her pants were on backwards, both legs were shoved through a single hole of her underwear, and she was wearing only socks on her feet. This case was to become the largest, longest, and most highly publicized criminal investigation in Edmonton's history.

Physical Evidence Found

An autopsy concluded that Punky died from severe blunt force genital trauma and smothering. Punky's clothing was sent to the RCMP's crime lab in Vancouver. In an attempt to find DNA belonging to the culprit, from semen or blood residue, swabs from the victim's anus and vagina were taken. Also, three small sections of Punky's panties were analyzed. In 1992, testing only small sections of clothing rather than the entire garment was a standard procedure. To isolate any semen or blood, filter paper was pressed against each panty section. Then, each piece of filter paper and the swabs were tested for blood using the phenolphthalein regent test (See module 3, lesson 2.) and for semen using the Fast Blue test (See module 3, lesson 3.). Unfortunately, these tests yielded no blood or semen.

During an examination of Punky's body, a single pubic hair was found on her ankle. During analysis of the hair, RCMP forensic experts thought that a small number of follicle cells may be found on the root tip. These follicle cells could yield DNA; however, there was not enough for them to test. At that time, the RCMP were using the RFLP method of DNA analysis, which requires 5 000 to 50 000 cells. The RCMP informed the EPS to test the follicle cells for DNA. The PCR testing method had to be used, but they would not begin using the PCR analysis method until the summer of 1993. 

Collection of Suspect DNA

One of the few pieces of physical evidence they had was the pubic hair. Over the course of this long investigation, the EPS collected DNA samples from over 400 suspects in the hopes that they could be compared to any DNA found in the pubic hair.

At this time, Canada"s DNA data bank had not yet been established (2000), so EPS investigators had to ask the suspects to donate voluntarily their DNA. The majority of suspects asked were sex offenders because these types of criminals tend to re-offend repeatedly until they are caught.

Clifford Sleigh was actually one of the suspects who volunteered his DNA because he was previously arrested and convicted for sexually assaulting two teenage girls. Sleigh was sentenced to 15 years in prison for both crimes. In addition to asking Sleigh for his DNA, investigators asked for his alibi for the day Punky was killed. Sleigh said he was with his family that day. When police asked Sleigh's wife and several of his in-laws, they said he was with them all day. 

Bad News and Good News 

The EPS expected PCR testing of any DNA from the pubic hair left by Punky's killer to be done by the RCMP in 1993. However, the RCMP postponed the initiation of their PCR testing facilities until 1995 and were not able to analyze the hair for DNA until 1997. Throughout this time, EPS investigators patiently collected and investigated thousands of tips from the public with no successful results.

Finally in 1997, RCMP forensic experts used the PCR analysis technique to analyze the hair. After waiting four years for this test, EPS investigators were told that insufficient follicle cells were available on the hair root tip.

After a long period of no solid leads in the investigation of Punky's murder, advances in DNA testing resulted in some good news. In 2000, the EPS sent physical evidence from the case to a forensic lab in the United States that was able to do DNA testing that the RCMP was unable to do. Instead of testing for semen using small sections of Punky"s panties, US forensic scientists tested the entire garment and found some semen. They extracted DNA from the semen, and with the help of the PCR analysis method, they created a complete DNA profile for Punky's killer by 2001. 

Matching the Killer's DNA Profile

When the DNA profile of Punky's killer was sent to Canada's National DNA Data Bank for comparison, no match was found because Clifford Sleigh's DNA was not registered. The EPS then decided to compare the murderer's DNA profile with the DNA samples they began collecting in 1993. The EPS database contained over 400 "raw" DNA samples that had not been turned into full DNA profiles. Clifford Sleigh's DNA should have been within the EPS database. Ironically, none of it was left. All the DNA that Clifford had volunteered had been used during previous forensic testing. Because none of Sleigh's DNA was in the EPS database, no match to the DNA from Punky's killer was found.

In September of 2001, Clifford Sleigh was serving prison time in a minimum security prison in Hobbema, Alberta. Sleigh and another prisoner escaped and fled to British Columbia. Sleigh was eventually caught and sent back to Alberta in September 2002. Before he was sent back to prison, a sample of his blood was taken, a DNA profile was made, and it was registered in the National DNA data bank. In January 2003, the EPS was told that Clifford Sleigh's DNA was identical to the DNA profile of Punky's killer. The chance of a complete DNA match such as this occurring randomly were 1 in 25 trillion.

The Confession and Trial

When told about the DNA evidence, Clifford Sleigh confessed and told investigators he was out to punish someone that day. That someone was an innocent six year-old girl. Sleigh pleaded guilty to kidnapping and sexually assault; however, he did not admit to intentionally killing Punky. He tried to plead guilty to the less serious charge of manslaughter. The prosecution did not accept this guilty plea and put him on trial for first-degree murder.

After eight days of testimony and eleven hours of deliberation, in May 2005 a jury found Clifford Sleigh guilty of kidnapping, aggravated sexual assault, and first-degree murder. He was given a life sentence with no parole for 25 years.

"We caught him. She can rest in peace now.''

- Karen Vallette (Punky Gustavson"s mother)
