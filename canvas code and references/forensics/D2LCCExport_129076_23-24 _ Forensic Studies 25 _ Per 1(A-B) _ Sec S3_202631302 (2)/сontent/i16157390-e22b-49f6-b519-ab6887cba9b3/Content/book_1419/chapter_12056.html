Case Study: Identifying the Victims of 9-11

The largest single disaster that involved the use of mtDNA analysis to identify human remains was the tragedy of September 11, 2001.

The two burning towers of the World Trade Center after terrorists hijacked and flew two large commercial airplanes into them. Shortly after this photo was taken both towers collapsed.

 

Thousands of tissue fragments were collected from the rubble of the World Trade Center in New York City, New York, U.S.A., after it had been destroyed by terrorists. Using forensic mtDNA analysis, the New York Medical Examiner's Office was able to identify 1 592 out of the 2 749 victims from approximately 20 000 body parts and tissue fragments that were collected.

To link body parts or tissue fragments to individual victims, an mtDNA database was created. To create this mtDNA database, relatives of the victims were asked to donate blood samples from which their mtDNA was collected. Also, relatives were given the difficult task of trying to collect mtDNA samples from their missing family members on articles such as hairbrushes and toothbrushes.

Identification of large body parts such as skulls and hands were made rapidly using dental and fingerprint records. Identification of the smaller tissue fragments took longer because mtDNA analysis methods had to be used and, unfortunately, this did not always prove successful. Many of the victims' DNA within the tissue fragments were damaged by the heat of the explosions or humidity from the water used to douse the fires; other tissue fragments were simply too small.

In February 2005, the effort by the New York Medical Examiner's Office to identify the remaining tissue samples ended because the samples left were either too small or too badly damaged. Over 9 000 small unidentified pieces of bone and tissue were sealed and stored in the hope that in the future a technology will be developed to identify these remains.

Identifying Victims from the Pentagon

Less than an hour after the two towers of the World Trade Center were hit by two commercial airplanes, the Pentagon was hit by a hijacked Boeing 757 commercial airplane in a related terrorist attack. The Pentagon, located a short distance from Washington, D.C., in Arlington, Virginia, is the headquarters of the U.S Department of Defense. The crash caused part of the building to collapse, killing 125 Pentagon personal and all 64 passengers aboard the plane.

The Office of the Armed Forces Medical Examiner in Washington, D.C., was in charge of the identification of the victims from this tragedy. Over 2000 separate tissue fragments were analyzed and identified by a large team of military experts. The task of identifying the human remains from the Pentagon was relatively easy compared to the identification of remains from the World Trade Center. The grim process took a relatively short period of time--a little over two and one-half months. This was not because there were fewer people killed; rather, DNA samples for the majority of the victims were available on a database prior to the incident because the majority of victims were military personal from the Pentagon whose dental and DNA records were readily accessible.


Mitochondrial DNA analysis evidence helped to convict Scott Peterson of killing his 27-year-old wife and their unborn son in Modesto, California, U.S.A.. Mitochondrial DNA extracted from a human hair shaft found in a pair of pliers in Peterson's boat matched his wife's mtDNA.
