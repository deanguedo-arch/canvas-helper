Description of DNA

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is found inside each of the 46 chromosomes within the nucleus of a normal human cell. DNA has a unique double helix molecular arrangement. It looks like a "twisted ladder". Although it is only a few microns wide, there is so much of it that when uncoiled it can stretch up to two metres! DNA consists of two matching strands fused together with hydrogen bonds. The precious interior of this "twisted ladder" is composed of four nucleic acid bases that are repeated over and over within the DNA strand. In fact, each DNA strand has some 3 billion repeating nucleic acid bases.

The names of the 4 nucleic acid bases within the chromosomes are adenine (A), thymine (T), cytosine (C), and guanine (G). Nucleic acid bases are found in pairs in the interior of the DNA strand. Because of the unique shapes of each of the nucleic acid bases, only certain bases will pair with each other.

  • Adenine will pair only with thymine (A - T).
  • Cytosine will pair only with guanine (C - G).

The distinct sequence of these nucleic acids creates amino acids. As you can see in the image above, a nucleic acid sequence of GCA corresponds to the alanine amino acid (Ala), the AGA sequence to the arginine amino acid (Arg), and so on. In fact, 20 different amino acids in the human body can be combined to create thousands of different proteins.

DNA left on cigarette butts inside the apartment of a mother and daughter murdered in 1993 was linked to a 44-year-old man in Wisconsin who was in prison for attempted murder. The man was charged with two counts of murder based this evidence.
