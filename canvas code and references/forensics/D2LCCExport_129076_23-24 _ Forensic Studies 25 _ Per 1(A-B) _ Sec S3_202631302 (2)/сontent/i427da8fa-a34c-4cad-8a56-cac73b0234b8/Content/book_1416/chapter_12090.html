The ABO blood classification system classifies blood into different types: type A, B, AB and O.  The ABO system relies on using the proteins on the outside of red blood cells.  These proteins are called antigens.

There are only 2 types of antigens in the blood system: A and B.  Cells either have antigen A only, (Type A), antigen B only (Type B), antigen A and B (Type AB) or no antigens (Type O) for this system.

If a person does not normally produce an antigen, then their body will treat that antigen as an invader if it's introduced into their bloodstream.  The body will produce antibodies which attack this foreign antigen.  

These antibodies will clump (agglutinate) with your red blood cells and make it impossible for normal circulation to occur.  This is why you must receive a blood transfusion from someone with the same blood type as you.

In a lab, we can use these antibody reactions to determine what type of blood a person has.  It's possible to see the agglutination of blood cells under a microscope.
