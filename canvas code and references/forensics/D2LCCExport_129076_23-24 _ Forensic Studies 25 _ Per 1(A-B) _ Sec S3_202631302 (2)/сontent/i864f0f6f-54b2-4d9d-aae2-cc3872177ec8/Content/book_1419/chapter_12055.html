Case Study: Forensic Identification of Unknown Soldiers



Unquestionably, the largest ongoing scientific research program involving mtDNA analysis is occurring at the United States Defense Department's Joint POW/MIA Central Identification Laboratory in Rockville, Maryland, USA. The task of the forensic anthropologists and forensic odontologists working at this laboratory is to locate, exhume, and identify the missing human remains of American military members from Word War II, the Korean War, the Vietnam War, and the Gulf War. This is a huge task because approximately 45 000 soldiers are missing from these military conflicts. Since this program began in the 1990s, over 1 000 human remains have been identified and returned to their families.

The first step in the identification of recovered human remains of a military member is an examination by a forensic anthropologist. The amount of remains analyzed is usually dependent on how the soldier died. The skeletal remains of ground combat soldiers are more complete and better preserved than those remains of pilots whose aircraft crashed into the ground at high speeds. The remains are first laid out in anatomical order and any foreign debris is removed. After a forensic anthropologist examines the size, density, and other characteristics, he or she can often hypothesize the age, gender, and race and even identify some medical disorders of the deceased person.

After examination by the forensic anthropologist, any teeth found are examined and possibly identified by a forensic odontologist. This involves a computer database containing thousands of dental records from soldiers (excluding World War II soldiers). If dental identification cannot be completed, an mtDNA analysis done. The mtDNA taken from teeth, bones, or hair from the remains are analyzed, compared, and matched to samples from an mtDNA database. The database used contains mtDNA reference samples donated by maternal family members of missing military members of soldiers from World War II, the Korean War, or Vietnam War.

If the remains are from recent times (such as the Gulf War or the Iraqi War), the mtDNA is compared to entries in a large database of mtDNA samples from the soldiers themselves. Since the late-1980s, DNA samples have been taken from every military member for identification purposes. Currently, this database contains over 4 million nuclear DNA and mtDNA samples from military members and private citizens or contractors working for the US Defense Department in foreign countries. The existence of this DNA database will likely lead to no more remains from military members being classified as being "unknown".

mtDNA Identification and the Unknown Vietnam Soldier


Tomb of the Unknowns at Arlington National Cemetery. Photo courtesy of Sergeant A. Kowalyk (2006)

The Tomb of the Unknowns in Arlington, Virginia, U.S.A., is a popular tourist destination. The Tomb contains the remains of unknown American soldiers from World War I, World War II, the Korean War, and the Vietnam War and is guarded 24-hours-a-day every day of the year by specially trained military members. 

The power of mtDNA print analysis was demonstrated in 1998 when the remains of an unknown soldier from the Vietnam War were exhumed from the Tomb of the Unknowns. A forensic team matched mtDNA from the unknown remains with a donated mtDNA sample from one of the many families of missing relatives. Twenty-six years after he was shot down in Vietnam, the remains of Air Force Lieutenant Michael Blassie were identified. Testing of mtDNA was not available when Blassie was shot down in 1972 during a combat mission in Vietnam or in1984 when he was placed in the Tomb of the Unknowns.

Shortly after they were identified, Michael Blassie's remains were moved to a cemetery near his home in St. Louis, Missouri. The Vietnam crypt in the Tomb of the Unknowns is now empty.


In July of 1991, mtDNA analysis was used to identify nine bodies exhumed from a shallow grave in Russia. All of the skeletal remains were identified as members of the Romanov family, the last of the Russian royal family executed in 1918 by the Bolsheviks. Identification of the Romanov family was made after by comparing an mtDNA sample from a living Romanov descendent, Prince Philip, the Duke of Edinburgh (married currently to Queen Elizabeth) with the mtDNA from the bones of the nine skeletons.
