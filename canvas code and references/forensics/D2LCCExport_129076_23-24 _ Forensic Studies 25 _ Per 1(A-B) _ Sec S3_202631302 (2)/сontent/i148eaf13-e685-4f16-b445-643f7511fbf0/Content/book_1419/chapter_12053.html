The Function of the Mitochondria



Mitochondria, found in the cytoplasm of the cell, are the second largest organelles in the cell. They operate independently with little interaction with other organelles in the cell. Shaped like kidney beans, mitochondria are responsible for cellular respiration, the production of energy. Mitochondria contain enzymes that convert food material into adenosine triphosphate (ATP) that is used by all the cell organelles as an energy source. ATP is the energy molecule required for all cellular activities; every cell requires ATP to survive. In motile cells or cells that move (such as sperm cells), mitochondria are concentrated near moving structures (such as flagellum) because they require large amounts of energy to move.

The Discovery of Mitochondrial DNA

Previous to the early 1960s, it was thought that the DNA in a cell was only in the nucleus. However, in the early 1960s, mitochondria were discovered to have a small amount of DNA. This genetic material in the mitochondria became known as mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA). The structure of mtDNA is different from the DNA in the nucleus (nuclear DNA). It is seen in unorganized looping chains instead of within chromosomes.

A human ovum has more mitochondria than sperm cells have. This is because ova are much larger than sperm cells and because sperm cells need just enough energy to power them on their journey to an ovum. Immediately after a sperm penetrates an ovum (conception), the mitochondria in the sperm are destroyed leaving only the mother's mitochondria in the zygote. This is why 100% of the mtDNA in human cells comes from the mother. 

Your mtDNA is gained through maternal inheritance; therefore, your mtDNA is identical to your mother's mtDNA. All children who share the same biological mother have the same mtDNA. In fact, your mtDNA is identical to anyone from the maternal side or mother's side of your family.

In the above four generation pedigree, all the family members represented by a shaded circle or square have the same mtDNA. All the females pass their mtDNA to their children, but all the males who have children do not pass on their mtDNA. Therefore, the children do not have the same mtDNA as their fathers. In contrast, nuclear DNA is different in that half of the chromosomes in the nuclei are inherited from the mother and half of the chromosomes in the nuclei are inherited from the father.

The discovery of maternally inherited mtDNA has proven extremely useful in forensic investigations when unrecognizable human remains have been found. Comparison of the mtDNA from the remains with living maternal family members can confirm the identity of the deceased individual. Mitochondrial DNA analysis is completed in some criminal cases when no nuclear DNA is found in the biological evidence recovered.

Mitochondrial DNA was used to identify a direct descendent of the oldest skeletal remains found in Britain. The skeleton was that of a 23-year-old male who died from a blow to the head. The living descendent was a 42-year-old history teacher who lived just a short distance away from where the bones were first found in 1903. Mitochondrial DNA extracted from a tooth cavity in the 9,000 year old skeleton was used to link the two distant relatives.
